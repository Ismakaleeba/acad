Part I

WHAT ACAD IS

Applied Comparative Action Debate (ACAD) Official Manual – narrated, deep edition

A strategic introduction to this manual

This manual was not written to teach you how to win rounds.

It was written to change what debate produces.

Most debate manuals teach: how to structure speeches, how to clash, how to refute, how to persuade judges.

This manual teaches something more dangerous:

how to design real solutions inside real systems – under pressure.

If you are new to debate, this book will train you to think faster and deeper than most students your age.

If you are an experienced debater, this book will challenge some of your strongest habits.

You will realise very quickly that ACAD does not reward what traditional debate rewards.

It rewards something else: clarity of systems, honesty about implementation, and courage to quantify your impact.

Read this manual as if you are being trained for a real responsibility.

Because that is exactly what ACAD prepares you for.

1. What is Debate in ACAD?

Before you understand what ACAD is, you must be very clear about what debate traditionally is.

Not in theory.

In practice.

1.1 What debate traditionally is

In most formats around the world, debate is a competitive speaking activity built around defending a side of a statement.

The structure changes – but the logic does not.

Whether you debate:

  • policy motions,

  • parliamentary motions,

  • values motions,

  • or institutional simulations,

the heart of the activity is the same:

You are given a claim about the world.

You are assigned a side.

You build arguments that justify that side.

You clash with the other side’s arguments.

You try to persuade a judge that your side should win.

As debaters, we become extremely good at:

  • finding evidence quickly,

  • constructing logical narratives,

  • exposing contradictions,

  • reframing impacts,

  • and collapsing debates strategically.

But notice something important.

We are rarely required to explain how the world actually functions in operational detail.

We only need enough understanding to justify a position.

Let us be honest with ourselves.

In a policy round, you can defend a national healthcare reform without ever explaining:

  • how clinics are staffed,

  • how procurement happens,

  • how patients are registered,

  • how monitoring works,

  • or how budgets move through ministries.

In a parliamentary round, you can defend a social intervention without ever explaining:

  • who runs it,

  • who enforces it,

  • who pays for it,

  • or who maintains it after launch.

In a Model United Nations conference, you can negotiate a resolution without ever being responsible for its execution.

This is not a criticism.

It is simply the design of those formats.

They train persuasion.

They do not train implementation.

1.2 Why ACAD exists

ACAD exists because modern global problems do not fail at the level of ideas.

They fail at the level of systems.

Education reform fails not because people oppose education.

Youth employment programmes fail not because people dislike youth.

Climate action fails not because people deny climate change.

They fail because:

  • delivery chains break,

  • incentives misalign,

  • institutions do not coordinate,

  • data systems collapse,

  • budgets do not arrive,

  • and accountability is weak.

As debaters, we are trained to critique policies.

But we are almost never trained to design delivery.

ACAD was created to fill that gap.

It was created to transform debate from:

a competition of positions into a competition of systems.

ACAD starts from a very simple belief:

Students can understand complex sectors.

Students can design serious interventions.

Students can learn implementation logic long before they enter universities, governments or startups.

But only if debate is redesigned.

1.3 How ACAD is different

ACAD does not replace debate.

It redefines what debating means.

To understand that shift clearly, it is useful to compare ACAD with the formats you already know.

ACAD and Policy Debate

Policy debate is built around a single idea:

Should a specific actor adopt a specific policy?

The affirmative designs a policy.

The negative attacks the policy using disadvantages, counterplans, topicality and critiques.

The debate focuses on:

  • comparative advantages,

  • risk calculus,

  • and argumentative strategy.

ACAD removes one critical element:

There is no fixed policy actor.

There is no obligation to defend one governmental action.

Both teams are designers.

Both teams must build an intervention system.

The clash is not:

policy versus no policy.

It is:

system design versus system design.

More importantly, ACAD shifts attention away from theoretical feasibility and towards operational realism.

In policy debate, a plan can survive if it is theoretically possible.

In ACAD, a design must survive contact with implementation detail.

ACAD and BP / WSD

British Parliamentary and World Schools formats are built around persuasion under time pressure.

The core skill is comparative narrative framing.

Teams are rewarded for:

  • originality,

  • strategic extensions,

  • principled analysis,

  • and persuasive delivery.

ACAD deliberately reduces the importance of narrative framing.

Instead of asking:

Which story about the world is more compelling?

ACAD asks:

Which system intervention functions more reliably?

A beautiful speech that cannot explain delivery chains will lose to a technically accurate but simply delivered design.

ACAD and Model United Nations

MUN trains institutional awareness and diplomacy.

Delegates represent countries or organisations.

They negotiate text.

They seek consensus.

They simulate political reality.

ACAD removes representation.

Students do not speak as Rwanda, the UN, the African Union or ministries.

They speak as designers operating within real systems.

The objective is not to pass a resolution.

The objective is to build an intervention architecture that could realistically be deployed.

ACAD therefore avoids the performative politics of institutional role-play and focuses instead on structural problem-solving.

1.4 The core idea of ACAD

ACAD is built around one idea:

Debating is not about defending positions.

It is about comparing real systems and real actions.

In ACAD, you do not defend a side.

You defend a design.

You do not attack a claim.

You stress-test a delivery mechanism.

You do not win by out-speaking.

You win by out-building.

When an ACAD judge listens to you, they are not asking:

“Is this persuasive?”

They are asking:

“If I were forced to choose one of these systems to implement, which one would fail less?”

2. The ACAD Culture

(Non-negotiable principles)

This chapter defines the soul of ACAD.

These principles are not style preferences.

They are enforcement rules.

If a round violates them, it is no longer ACAD.

2.1 Show, don’t argue

In ACAD, a claim that cannot be shown is incomplete.

Consider this sentence:

“Youth lack access to tourism opportunities.”

In ACAD, this is only the beginning of your work.

You must show:

  • where information about tourism jobs is created,

  • how it travels to young people,

  • which gatekeepers control entry,

  • which qualifications are recognised,

  • and which steps block access.

A judge should be able to trace a real pathway:

from a young person to an employer through your system.

A useful self-test:

If you removed your voice and left only your Action Board behind, would your solution still be understandable?

If the answer is no, you are still arguing.

2.2 Systems first, action second

This rule exists to prevent superficial innovation.

ACAD requires you to begin with a baseline system.

Before you design anything, you must explain:

Who does what today?

Who decides?

Who pays?

Who benefits?

Who is excluded?

Where does coordination fail?

For example, in a tourism challenge in Rwanda, a serious ACAD team must understand:

  • how TVET institutions interact with tourism employers,

  • how certification is recognised,

  • how district tourism offices function,

  • how private operators recruit,

  • and how donor projects currently intervene.

Only after that can an intervention be justified.

An action that ignores existing structures is not innovative.

It is disconnected.

2.3 Evidence and logic over rhetoric

In ACAD, evidence is not used to prove that a problem matters.

It is used to prove that your design can operate.

When you claim:

“Employers will participate,”

you must justify that claim using:

  • cost structures,

  • incentive alignment,

  • regulatory requirements,

  • or demonstrated behaviour.

Logic replaces persuasion.

Assumptions must be exposed.

A good ACAD judge listens for missing logic, not missing passion.

2.4 Measurable impact

ACAD treats impact as a technical question.

Not a rhetorical one.

You must specify:

  • what outcome changes,

  • for whom,

  • how much,

  • and by when.

For example:

Not:

“Youth empowerment improves.”

But:

“Six months after placement, retention rates among participating youth increase from X to Y.”

If you cannot specify what success looks like, your intervention cannot be evaluated.

2.5 Scalability

ACAD distinguishes between pilots and systems.

A pilot tests an idea.

A system changes a sector.

Your design must show:

  • how additional users are added,

  • how resources grow,

  • how coordination remains stable,

  • and how costs behave as scale increases.

If your intervention works only under ideal conditions, it is not a competitive ACAD design.

2.6 Context sensitivity – local reality matters

ACAD strongly rejects copy-paste solutions.

An intervention that works in one country, one city or one institution cannot be assumed to work elsewhere.

Teams must demonstrate understanding of:

  • legal environments,

  • cultural norms,

  • institutional capacity,

  • infrastructure,

  • and economic constraints.

In Rwanda’s tourism sector, for example, it matters:

  • who regulates tour operators,

  • how decentralised governance operates,

  • how language and certification interact with employment,

  • and how donor programmes shape the market.

ACAD treats context as part of the system.

Not as background.

2.7 Implementation realism

This is the final and most unforgiving principle.

Every ACAD design must answer six operational questions:

Implementation Question Strategic Focus
Who executes this? Delivery agents and institutional leads.
Who funds this? Budget sources and financial sustainability.
Who governs this? Authority, oversight, and reporting lines.
Who monitors this? Continuous data collection and verification.
Who corrects failure? Accountability loops and contingency protocols.
Who sustains this? Long-term operational and resource stability.

If any of these questions is unanswered, the design is structurally incomplete.

A common mistake among excellent debaters is to assume that implementation is easy once the idea is accepted.

ACAD is built on the opposite assumption:

Implementation is the hardest part.

And therefore, it must be debated.

This is what ACAD is.

Not a format.

A discipline.

PART II – ACAD Motions & Scope

How problems enter the room — and how teams are allowed to touch them

Before a single speaker stands up. Before an action board is designed. Before a judge becomes an Impact Panelist.

There is one quiet but powerful moment that defines the entire round:

the motion.

In ACAD, the motion is not a topic. It is a design brief for reality.

This chapter teaches you how ACAD motions work, how they differ from traditional debate motions, and—most importantly—what teams are allowed and not allowed to do with them.

If you misunderstand this part, everything else in ACAD collapses into normal debating.

3. ACAD Motions

3.1 What makes a motion an ACAD motion

An ACAD motion is not asking:

Should we do something?

It is asking:

How should real people design a real system to solve a real problem in a real place?

A motion becomes an ACAD motion when it satisfies all four filters below.

Filter 1 — It must be a real problem

Not a philosophical dilemma. Not a values clash. Not a moral framing.

It must be a problem that already exists in the world.

Example:

❌ “This House believes education is the most important driver of development.” (Too abstract. No system. No design challenge.)

✅ “How can Rwanda’s tourism sector design systems to increase youth participation in the tourism value chain?”

You can point to this problem in real life. You can meet the people affected by it.

That is ACAD.

Filter 2 — It must require a system, not a single action

ACAD is allergic to one-off interventions.

If a solution can be summarized as:

  • “build a center”

  • “run a campaign”

  • “pass a law”

…then the motion is not being treated properly.

An ACAD motion forces teams to think in:

  • pipelines

  • institutions

  • incentives

  • coordination

  • delivery chains

  • feedback loops

If your solution fits on one slide, you have not yet built a system.

Filter 3 — It must force implementation

ACAD does not allow “someone should do something”.

The motion must force teams to confront:

  • who acts

  • who pays

  • who manages

  • who trains

  • who regulates

  • who evaluates

  • who absorbs failure

If you cannot assign responsibility to real stakeholders, the motion is being escaped.

Filter 4 — It must require measurable and scalable impact

ACAD does not reward “good ideas”.

It rewards:

  • repeatable outcomes

  • expansion potential

  • cost awareness

  • reach

If your solution only works for:

  • one school

  • one district

  • one NGO pilot

…without a path to scale, it is incomplete.

In one sentence:

An ACAD motion is a structured challenge to design, justify and compare real-world delivery systems.

3.2 The difference between: Policy motion, ACAD challenge, and ACAD motion

Understanding this distinction is essential, especially for debaters coming from Policy, BP, or MUN.

Let’s walk through the difference slowly.

A. Policy motion

A policy motion usually looks like:

This House would implement X policy.

The debate becomes:

  • is the policy good or bad?

  • what are its impacts?

  • what are its disadvantages?

The core skill tested is: argument clash.

The policy itself is often treated as a black box.

B. ACAD challenge

An ACAD challenge is a raw problem statement used in training or innovation labs.

Example:

“Low youth participation in Rwanda’s tourism value chain.”

It is not yet a debate motion. It is a problem prompt.

It defines:

  • the pain

  • the context

  • the development gap

But it does not yet structure competition.

Think of it as a briefing document.

C. ACAD motion

An ACAD motion converts the challenge into a design and comparison task.

It asks:

How can stakeholders design scalable systems to solve X in Y context?

For example:

How can public and private stakeholders design scalable systems to increase youth participation in Rwanda’s tourism value chain?

Now it becomes competitive because:

  • both teams must design full systems

  • both teams must defend feasibility

  • both teams must compare delivery outcomes

ACAD is not:

who has better arguments.

It is:

who has the better system.

Short comparison

Format What is being compared? What wins
Policy Claims and impacts Better argument strategy
ACAD Systems and delivery models Better real-world design

3.3 The standard ACAD motion structure

All official ACAD motions follow one core structure:

How can [stakeholders] design scalable systems to solve [problem] in [context]?

Let’s unpack each component carefully.

1. “How can”

This removes moral posturing.

You are not being asked:

  • whether the problem matters

  • whether it is sad

  • whether it deserves attention

It already does.

You are being asked: how to act.

2. “stakeholders”

ACAD never centers a single heroic actor.

Stakeholders may include:

  • government ministries

  • private sector actors

  • schools and training institutions

  • community organizations

  • international partners

  • regulators

  • financial institutions

The motion intentionally keeps this plural.

Why?

Because ACAD believes real development is coordinated.

3. “design scalable systems”

This is the philosophical heart of ACAD.

Not:

  • launch a project

  • create an initiative

  • introduce a reform

But: design a system.

A system has:

  • inputs

  • processes

  • governance

  • incentives

  • monitoring

  • correction mechanisms

  • scaling pathways

If your model does not have internal structure, it is not a system.

4. “solve [problem]”

ACAD does not reward reframing the problem away.

You must directly engage the core failure.

If the problem is: low youth participation in tourism,

You cannot escape by talking only about:

  • general youth empowerment

  • entrepreneurship broadly

  • mindset change

You must explain how youth enter, stay, grow and advance in the tourism value chain.

5. “in [context]”

Context is not decoration.

It is binding.

An ACAD motion without context becomes generic development talk.

Context determines:

  • institutional capacity

  • legal frameworks

  • labor market structure

  • infrastructure

  • education pipeline

  • political realities

In ACAD: local reality matters.

3.4 Scope rules

This section is one of the most important protections of ACAD culture.

Without scope rules, teams start escaping into fantasy.

3.4.1 What teams may assume

Teams may assume only what is structurally reasonable for the context.

This includes:

  • the existence of current institutions (e.g. ministries, tourism boards, vocational schools, private companies)

  • normal levels of political stability (unless the motion explicitly involves crisis or conflict)

  • realistic budget expansion (not unlimited money, not zero funding either)

  • willingness of stakeholders to cooperate at a basic level (not perfect alignment, not sabotage by default)

You may assume: the system can be attempted.

You may not assume: the system will magically succeed.

3.4.2 What teams must justify

Every ACAD team must justify:

1. Stakeholder selection

Why these actors? Why not others? Who leads? Who coordinates?

2. Delivery pathway

How does a beneficiary move through your system?

From: no access → entry → training → placement → retention → growth

3. Resource flow

Where does funding come from? How is it allocated? What happens when funding drops?

4. Capacity building

Who trains whom? At what scale? With what quality control?

5. Incentive alignment

Why would:

  • firms participate?

  • institutions cooperate?

  • young people stay in the system?

6. Monitoring and correction

How do you detect failure? How do you redesign? Who is accountable?

7. Scaling logic

What changes when the system grows? What breaks first? What must be decentralized?

3.4.3 What teams are not allowed to assume

The following are strictly prohibited assumptions in ACAD:

  • perfect governance

  • corruption-free implementation by default

  • unlimited donor funding

  • universal community buy-in

  • instant behavior change

  • flawless technology adoption

If your model collapses when real human behavior appears, it is not an ACAD system.

3.5 The boundary between creativity and realism

ACAD encourages innovation.

But innovation must remain deployable.

A simple test used by experienced ACAD judges:

Could a ministry, NGO, company or school reasonably pilot this within 12–24 months?

If the answer is clearly no, your solution is drifting into speculative policy.

3.6 A worked example – tourism youth participation

Let us briefly illustrate how scope applies.

Motion:

How can stakeholders design scalable systems to increase youth participation in Rwanda’s tourism value chain?

A weak scope interpretation would be:

“We create a national youth tourism academy and run awareness campaigns.”

This fails because:

  • no delivery chain is shown

  • no integration with employers

  • no absorption capacity

  • no long-term pipeline

A stronger ACAD interpretation would:

  • map how secondary schools feed into vocational tourism training

  • integrate private lodges and tour operators as training hosts

  • design wage co-financing or apprenticeship incentives

  • embed certification recognized by employers

  • create regional rollout logic

  • include placement tracking and retention feedback

Notice:

The solution is not flashy.

It is operational.

That is ACAD.

3.7 The philosophical shift ACAD motions impose

Traditional debate asks you:

What should the world do?

ACAD asks you:

How does the world actually change?

That shift forces debaters to become:

  • designers

  • implementers

  • systems thinkers

  • delivery strategists

Not just speakers.

Remember this line. It defines the entire format.

In ACAD, motions do not invite opinions. They demand architectures of change.

When you walk into an ACAD round, you are not walking into a debate.

You are walking into a design contest for reality.

4. Understanding the Problem Space (Systems Thinking)

Why ACAD does not let you fix broken outcomes without fixing the machine that creates them

Before you design a solution in ACAD… before you touch your Action Board… before you even choose your stakeholders…

You must answer one quiet but brutal question:

What is the system that keeps producing this problem?

Most debaters rush to solutions because debating culture trains you to respond fast.

ACAD trains you to look longer.

This chapter teaches you how to see the problem the way a delivery engineer, a policymaker and a systems designer would see it — not the way a speaker sees a motion.

4.1 What “the system” means in ACAD

In ACAD, a system is:

the full structure of people, rules, money, information, incentives and processes that repeatedly produces the same outcome.

Not once. Not accidentally. Repeatedly.

A system is not an organisation. It is not a ministry. It is not a program.

It is the invisible machine behind them.

Let’s make this very concrete.

When you hear:

“Youth are not participating in the tourism value chain.”

Your instinct as a debater is to think:

They lack skills. They lack awareness. They lack access.

ACAD stops you and asks:

Why does the country keep producing young people who cannot enter tourism?

That “why” is the system.

A system includes:

  • who controls entry

  • who defines qualifications

  • who pays for training

  • who bears risk

  • who hires

  • who certifies

  • who filters

  • who monitors

  • who replaces failure

If you cannot see these relationships, you are not yet seeing the system.

In ACAD, we use one simple rule:

If removing one organisation does not break your model, you probably described an activity — not a system.

4.2 Why ACAD forces systems thinking

Here is a painful truth.

Most development failures do not come from bad intentions. They come from:

systems that quietly block delivery.

You can train 5,000 young people.

But if:

  • employers do not trust the certification

  • hotels cannot afford new staff

  • recruitment happens through informal networks

  • rural youth cannot relocate

  • contracts are seasonal and unstable

…the system will eat your intervention.

ACAD exists because traditional debating celebrates ideas that would collapse the moment they meet reality.

ACAD does not reward “smart proposals”.

It rewards: designs that survive friction.

4.3 Mapping the system

In ACAD, every team must be able to mentally map five elements:

  • actors

  • flows

  • constraints

  • incentives

  • decision points

This is not a visual exercise only. It is a thinking discipline.

Let’s walk through them.

4.3.1 Actors

Actors are not “stakeholders” in a vague sense.

Actors are:

entities that have the power to move, block or distort outcomes.

In the tourism youth example, actors may include:

  • training institutions

  • tourism businesses

  • local governments

  • national tourism authorities

  • financing bodies

  • community leaders

  • youth themselves

But ACAD does not stop at listing.

You must ask:

Who controls:

  • admission?

  • hiring?

  • certification?

  • funding?

  • standards?

  • access to infrastructure?

Two actors with the same title do not have the same power.

A small hotel owner and a national tourism board are not symmetric.

ACAD trains you to look at authority, not labels.

4.3.2 Flows

Flows are what move through the system.

In most ACAD problems, the main flows are:

  • people

  • money

  • information

  • credentials

  • opportunities

You must trace them.

Example:

A young person:

Where do they come from? Which school? Which district? Which background?

How do they discover tourism careers? Who informs them?

How do they enter training? Who selects them?

How do they reach employers? Who connects them?

How do employers evaluate them? What proof do they trust?

If you cannot draw the journey of one beneficiary through your system, your model is incomplete.

4.3.3 Constraints

Constraints are the hard walls of reality.

Not preferences. Not excuses. Not political rhetoric.

Constraints are:

  • limited budgets

  • limited trainers

  • limited infrastructure

  • legal restrictions

  • geographic distance

  • language barriers

  • institutional capacity

ACAD requires you to acknowledge them before you design around them.

This is critical:

A solution that ignores constraints is not ambitious. It is irresponsible.

4.3.4 Incentives

Incentives are the real drivers of behaviour.

Not mission statements. Not press releases. Not values.

People respond to:

  • profit

  • risk

  • workload

  • reputation

  • career survival

  • political reward

  • organisational performance metrics

For example:

Why would a hotel train young workers instead of experienced staff?

Why would a training institution adjust its curriculum?

Why would a ministry continue funding your system when a new minister arrives?

If your system only works because people are “committed to development”, it will not survive.

ACAD designs for self-interest aligned with public good.

4.3.5 Decision points

Every system has gates.

Points where someone says yes or no.

Admission. Hiring. Certification. Funding approval. Licensing. Deployment.

ACAD solutions must identify:

Where are the gates? Who controls them? How do you change what happens at those gates?

4.4 Root cause vs surface problem

This is where most debaters fail.

They fix what is visible.

ACAD fixes what produces what is visible.

Surface problem:

Youth are unemployed in tourism.

Possible root causes:

  • training content does not match industry needs

  • employers do not trust existing certificates

  • rural youth cannot afford relocation

  • recruitment relies on personal networks

  • small tourism businesses cannot absorb training costs

  • seasonal demand discourages long-term contracts

If you treat unemployment as the root problem, your solution will be shallow.

If you treat pipeline failure as the root problem, your solution becomes structural.

Here is a powerful ACAD test:

If your intervention succeeds perfectly, what still remains broken?

If the answer is: “Most of the pipeline still works the same way” …then you treated the symptom.

4.5 Why ACAD forbids single-action solutions

ACAD formally forbids solutions that can be reduced to:

  • one program

  • one law

  • one institution

  • one campaign

  • one training intervention

Why?

Because single actions do not repair broken systems.

They only decorate them.

Let’s illustrate.

Suppose a team proposes:

“We introduce a national tourism skills bootcamp.”

On its own, this does not answer:

  • Who absorbs graduates?

  • Who validates quality?

  • Who funds repeat cohorts?

  • Who aligns curriculum with industry change?

  • Who places graduates?

  • Who tracks retention?

  • Who scales delivery beyond pilot cities?

Without these links, the bootcamp becomes a bottleneck, not a solution.

In ACAD, a solution must survive the following question:

What happens before this action, and what must happen after it?

If you cannot answer both, you do not yet have a system.

4.6 The ACAD chain rule

Every legitimate ACAD solution must create a continuous chain:

entry → preparation → transition → absorption → retention → progression

Breaking any link breaks the outcome.

Most debates focus only on: preparation.

ACAD focuses on: the full delivery chain.

4.7 A short story: why this matters

A real development practitioner once described a youth employment program that trained thousands of young people.

The evaluation report looked excellent.

Completion rates were high. Participant satisfaction was high.

But six months later, employment levels were unchanged.

Why?

Because employers had never been part of the system.

The program fixed a learning gap.

The system still contained a hiring gate that never moved.

This is exactly the kind of failure ACAD is designed to expose in debate.

4.8 How this changes your preparation as a debater

In traditional formats, you ask:

What are my arguments?

In ACAD, you must first ask:

What is the machine producing this problem?

Your preparation should begin with:

  • mapping actors

  • tracing flows

  • identifying bottlenecks

  • locating decision gates

  • testing incentive alignment

Only after that do you design actions.

This is why ACAD teaches:

Systems first. Action second.

4.9 The ACAD mental shift

Do not enter an ACAD round trying to impress.

Enter it trying to understand.

Your credibility does not come from how fast you speak.

It comes from how accurately you diagnose reality.

Remember this:

In ACAD, solutions are judged by how well they fit the system — not by how exciting they sound.

If you learn to see the system clearly, your actions will start to make sense automatically.

That is the foundation of real ACAD debating.

PART III – Roles, Vocabulary & Actors

5. Teams and Sides

(Why ACAD deliberately deletes “Government” and “Opposition” — and what replaces them)

Before we even teach you how to speak in ACAD, we must first clean something very deep out of your debating instinct.

If you come from BP, WSD or Policy, you have been trained to think like this:

Who am I? Am I Government or Opposition?

ACAD quietly removes that question.

Not because those formats are wrong — but because they train you to defend positions, not to design reality.

In ACAD, there is no side that is “for” and no side that is “against”.

There are only teams trying to build something that actually works.

5.1 The most important change in ACAD

In ACAD, both teams are solution teams.

Always.

There is no attacking team. There is no blocking team. There is no denial team.

There are only:

two competing designs for solving the same real problem.

This is why ACAD uses only two side names:

➤ Team A – Solution Side

➤ Team B – Solution Side

They are intentionally identical.

Same rights. Same responsibilities. Same burden.

The only difference between them is:

what they build.

If you remember only one sentence from this chapter, remember this:

ACAD is not “Are we right or wrong?” It is “Which system works better?”

5.2 Why ACAD kills Government vs Opposition

Let us be honest with ourselves as debaters.

The moment a round says:

“You are Opposition”

your brain stops thinking like a designer.

It starts thinking like a destroyer.

You begin to look for:

  • flaws to exaggerate,

  • assumptions to nitpick,

  • wording to trap,

  • rhetoric to weaken.

This is powerful for persuasion.

But it is terrible for innovation.

Real problems do not improve because someone “opposed” a solution well.

They improve because someone built a better one.

That is the cultural shift ACAD forces.

In ACAD:

You are never rewarded for proving that the other team is bad.

You are rewarded for showing that:

your system survives reality better than theirs.

5.3 What does “Solution Side” actually mean?

Being a Solution Side does not mean:

  • you only talk about your project

  • you ignore the other team

  • you present independently

It means something more precise.

A Solution Side is responsible for:

  • diagnosing the system,

  • designing an intervention,

  • delivering a working chain,

  • and comparing its design against another design.

So both teams must do two things:

1. Build

2. Compare

If you only build and never compare, you are incomplete.

If you only compare and never build, you are disqualified in spirit.

This is why we do not call the teams:

  • “Affirmative” and “Negative”

  • “Pro” and “Con”

  • “Government” and “Opposition”

Those words carry a culture of debate that ACAD is intentionally leaving behind.

5.4 How Team A and Team B are assigned

There is no ideological meaning.

There is no advantage.

There is no framing difference.

Teams are assigned purely for operational clarity.

The round is symmetric.

Both teams receive:

  • the same ACAD Challenge

  • the same preparation time

  • the same speaking structure

  • the same cross-examination time

  • the same judging standard

The competition is not procedural.

It is comparative.

5.5 The psychological difference you must feel as a debater

In a normal debate round, you walk in thinking:

I must defend this side.

In an ACAD round, you must walk in thinking:

I must design something that could actually be implemented.

That single change shifts everything:

  • how you research

  • how you select evidence

  • how you structure your speeches

  • how you respond in cross-examination

  • how you build your Action Board

You stop searching for “arguments”.

You start searching for:

failure points.

5.6 The real competition: model vs model

Let us ground this in your tourism example.

The challenge:

Design scalable systems to increase meaningful youth participation in Rwanda’s tourism value chain.

Team A may design:

  • a decentralised apprenticeship-to-hiring pipeline embedded in private hotels and lodges.

Team B may design:

  • a national youth tourism enterprise incubation and placement platform.

Both are valid. Both are solutions. Both are real designs.

The round is not:

Is youth participation important?

The round is:

Which system produces more youth employment, more sustainably, with less failure risk?

That is why both teams are Solution Sides.

5.7 What you are allowed to say about the other team

This is extremely important for ACAD culture.

You are not supposed to say:

  • “Their case is unrealistic.”

  • “Their plan is vague.”

  • “Their actors will not cooperate.”

Instead, you are trained to say:

  • “At this delivery gate, your system collapses because…”

  • “This incentive misalignment prevents scale because…”

  • “Your funding loop breaks after year one because…”

  • “Your placement chain does not absorb supply because…”

Notice the difference.

You are not attacking people.

You are stress-testing systems.

This protects ACAD from becoming:

a louder version of policy debate.

5.8 Why ACAD still allows comparison and pressure

Some debaters worry:

“If both of us are solution sides, how do we clash?”

The answer is:

You clash at the system level.

You compare:

  • leverage points

  • delivery chains

  • cost structures

  • institutional burden

  • political survivability

  • operational complexity

  • scaling constraints

  • failure recovery mechanisms

This is deeper than rebuttal.

It is design competition.

5.9 The equal burden rule

In ACAD:

Both teams carry the same burden.

There is no presumption.

There is no status quo advantage.

There is no “defending the world as it is”.

Every team must prove:

Our system improves the problem more reliably than the alternative.

If both systems are weak, judges must still choose:

the less fragile one.

5.10 A quiet but powerful rule

You are not allowed to reject the challenge itself.

You may not say:

  • “The problem is overstated.”

  • “The framing is wrong.”

  • “This is not the real issue.”

The ACAD Challenge defines the design space.

Your job is not to debate whether the problem exists.

Your job is to build within it.

This is another way ACAD protects itself from rhetorical escape.

5.11 Why this matters for your identity as a debater

ACAD does not train you to be:

a sharper speaker.

It trains you to be:

a better problem solver.

When you leave an ACAD round, the question should not be:

Did I win?

It should be:

Would my system survive a pilot?

That is why the only sides in ACAD are:

Team A – Solution Side Team B – Solution Side

No power roles. No political simulation. No institutional theatre.

Only:

design versus design.

6. Standard ACAD Speaker Roles (official vocabulary)

(Why every ACAD team is built like a real project team — not a debate team)

In ACAD, you do not walk into a round as “first speaker” or “second speaker”.

You walk in as part of a design team.

Each speaker carries a professional responsibility inside the solution.

This is intentional.

Because in the real world, no serious project is built by four people doing the same job.

ACAD mirrors how real systems are built.

That is why every team has four fixed roles.

Not titles for prestige. Roles for function.

Every ACAD team has:

  • System Lead

  • Design Lead

  • Delivery Lead

  • Impact Lead

These are the official ACAD role names. They must be used in all manuals, tournaments and training.

Before we explain each one, understand one important rule:

These roles define what you are responsible for, not only when you speak.

If a role fails, the entire system becomes weaker.

6.1 System Lead

(The person who proves that you understand the real world)

The System Lead is the anchor of the entire team.

If the System Lead fails, everything else becomes decoration.

What the System Lead actually does

The System Lead is responsible for:

  • explaining how the current system works

  • identifying the real bottlenecks

  • showing where change is possible

  • defining the problem in operational terms

This is not storytelling.

This is diagnosis.

Think of the System Lead as the person who walks into a hospital before surgery and says:

“This is where the damage actually is.”

In practice

The System Lead must clearly show:

  • the main actors

  • how people and resources move

  • where decisions are made

  • where the system breaks

They must answer questions such as:

  • Who controls access?

  • Who controls money?

  • Who controls standards?

  • Who controls hiring?

  • Who absorbs risk?

What the System Lead must not do

They must not jump to solutions.

In ACAD, proposing actions before explaining the system is a structural mistake.

The System Lead is not there to impress.

They are there to make reality visible.

The Action Board responsibility

The System Lead owns the system map section of the Action Board.

If judges cannot visually understand the system from your board after this speech, your team has already lost clarity.

The invisible test of a good System Lead

After this speech, judges should be able to answer:

“If I removed one institution, would the system collapse or adapt?”

If the judges cannot see that logic, the system was not properly explained.

6.2 Design Lead

(The person who builds the intervention — not the dream)

Once the system is understood, the Design Lead steps in.

This is the architect.

Not the motivator.

Not the visionary.

The builder.

What the Design Lead actually does

The Design Lead is responsible for:

  • choosing leverage points in the system

  • designing how the intervention interacts with existing actors

  • creating the internal logic of the solution

Their job is to answer:

Where do we touch the system so that the behaviour of the system changes?

In practice

The Design Lead must show:

  • which parts of the system are modified

  • which roles change

  • which processes are redesigned

  • which new links are created

  • which old links are removed

This is structural design.

A simple way to understand this role

If the System Lead shows you the broken machine…

The Design Lead redesigns the gears.

What the Design Lead must avoid

The Design Lead must not:

  • propose many disconnected actions

  • create parallel institutions without justification

  • solve everything at once

ACAD strongly prefers:

small number of well-placed interventions over large lists of programs.

The Action Board responsibility

The Design Lead owns:

  • the intervention architecture

  • the system changes layer

  • the leverage point layer

They must clearly show:

before → after

The invisible test of a good Design Lead

A judge should be able to point to your board and say:

“This exact change is what produces the new behaviour.”

If they cannot point to it, your design is vague.

6.3 Delivery Lead

(The person who turns a design into something that can survive reality)

This is the most underestimated role.

And in ACAD, often the most decisive one.

The Delivery Lead answers one brutal question:

If this had to start tomorrow, how would it actually run?

What the Delivery Lead actually does

The Delivery Lead is responsible for:

  • implementation pathways

  • institutional ownership

  • operational sequencing

  • resource mobilisation

  • governance and coordination

  • risk handling

This is not logistics.

This is operational architecture.

In practice

The Delivery Lead must show:

  • who executes each part

  • how funds flow

  • how staff are recruited

  • how partners are onboarded

  • how oversight works

  • how conflict is resolved

  • how failure is detected early

Why this role exists

In traditional debates, implementation is often hidden inside one sentence:

“The government will implement…”

ACAD does not allow that sentence.

Someone must own implementation logic.

That person is the Delivery Lead.

The Action Board responsibility

The Delivery Lead owns:

  • the delivery chain

  • the execution timeline

  • the governance structure

  • the risk and contingency layer

The invisible test of a good Delivery Lead

A judge should be able to imagine:

who wakes up on Monday morning and starts working on your system.

If the judge cannot see that person, delivery is imaginary.

6.4 Impact Lead

(The person who proves the solution is worth doing)

The Impact Lead closes the loop.

They are responsible for one thing:

making sure the solution actually produces value.

But not rhetorical value.

Measured value.

What the Impact Lead actually does

The Impact Lead is responsible for:

  • defining success metrics

  • showing attribution

  • explaining monitoring mechanisms

  • projecting scale pathways

  • comparing impact performance with the other team

This role is about discipline.

In practice

The Impact Lead must show:

  • what indicators change

  • how they are measured

  • how often they are measured

  • who collects data

  • how decisions respond to results

Why this role is critical in ACAD

Without impact logic, ACAD becomes just another idea competition.

The Impact Lead ensures:

the solution is judged by outcomes.

The Action Board responsibility

The Impact Lead owns:

  • the outcome layer

  • the metric layer

  • the feedback and learning loop

  • the scale pathway

The invisible test of a good Impact Lead

A judge should be able to answer:

“How do we know this worked?”

If the answer is emotional, the Impact Lead failed.

If the answer is operational and measurable, the team is credible.

6.5 How these roles work together

These four roles are not four speeches.

They are four layers of one system.

You should be able to trace a single straight line:

System Lead → Design Lead → Delivery Lead → Impact Lead

If any link is broken, your model collapses.

This is not hierarchy.

It is dependency.

6.6 A powerful habit for ACAD teams

During preparation, ACAD teams should physically sit in this order:

System → Design → Delivery → Impact

And repeatedly ask:

  • Does this design still fit the system?

  • Does this delivery still fit the design?

  • Does this impact logic still fit delivery?

This loop is how real project teams work.

ACAD trains you in the same way.

6.7 One last cultural rule

You are allowed to help each other.

But you are not allowed to blur responsibilities.

When judges ask:

“Where is this explained?”

It must be clear:

That belongs to the System Lead. That belongs to the Design Lead. That belongs to the Delivery Lead. That belongs to the Impact Lead.

Because ACAD does not reward speakers.

It rewards teams that behave like real solution builders.

7. Evaluators in ACAD

(Why judges in ACAD are not judges — and why that matters)

Before you learn how to win an ACAD round, you must first understand something very important:

In ACAD, the people sitting in front of you are not judges.

They are Evaluators.

That word is not cosmetic. It defines how the entire format works.

Official ACAD title

The official title is:

Evaluator

Not judge. Not adjudicator. Not panelist.

Evaluator.

Because their job is not to decide who spoke better.

Their job is to evaluate which solution performs better.

Who an Evaluator is

An ACAD Evaluator is:

a trained assessor of system feasibility, delivery realism, impact credibility and scalability.

They are not required to be professional economists, engineers or policymakers.

But they are trained to think like:

  • implementers,

  • program designers,

  • system analysts,

  • impact evaluators.

Their mindset is not:

“Which team convinced me?”

Their mindset is:

“If I had to choose one design to support tomorrow, which one would I trust?”

What Evaluators evaluate

Evaluators evaluate four dimensions only.

These are the only legitimate grounds for decision in ACAD.

1. Feasibility

Can this system actually be implemented inside the stated context?

Evaluators look for:

  • institutional capacity

  • legal and administrative realism

  • operational complexity

  • coordination burden

  • political and organisational survivability

A brilliant design that cannot survive the institutions that must carry it is not feasible.

2. Impact

Will the system actually change the target problem?

Evaluators look for:

  • clear outcome indicators

  • credible attribution

  • realistic magnitude of change

  • timeline of effects

  • avoidance of double counting and inflated claims

ACAD does not reward dramatic outcomes.

It rewards defensible ones.

3. Scalability

Can the system grow beyond a pilot?

Evaluators look for:

  • replication logic

  • resource intensity

  • institutional standardisation

  • geographic and demographic expansion constraints

  • dependency on rare talent or exceptional leadership

If your system only works when extraordinary people run it, it is not scalable.

4. System integrity

This is uniquely ACAD.

Evaluators examine:

  • whether the intervention fits the diagnosed system

  • whether incentives align

  • whether delivery chains are complete

  • whether feedback and correction loops exist

If a design breaks its own logic under pressure, it loses.

What Evaluators are not

This must be said clearly.

Evaluators are not:

  • rhetoric judges

  • performance judges

  • stylistic judges

  • speed judges

  • humour judges

They do not score:

  • confidence

  • emotional appeal

  • voice projection

  • charisma

  • dramatic framing

Those skills may help communication.

They do not decide outcomes.

In ACAD, clarity matters.

Performance does not.

What Evaluators listen for

Evaluators listen for:

  • causal language (“because”, “therefore”, “which leads to”, “only if”)

  • operational language (“implemented by”, “funded through”, “governed by”)

  • measurement language (“tracked by”, “reported quarterly”, “baseline compared to”)

They listen for design.

Not debate tricks.

How Evaluators use comparison

Evaluators never ask:

“Is this a good idea?”

They ask:

“Is this better than the alternative design presented today?”

This is extremely important.

Even a strong design can lose if the competing design:

  • has fewer failure points

  • scales faster

  • costs less per outcome

  • survives political and institutional change better

ACAD is comparative.

Not absolute.

What an Evaluator must always write in their decision

Every ACAD decision must be rigorously justified across four dimensions:

Decision Dimension Evaluator Focus
Operational Feasibility Can this system realistically function given the constraints?
Credible Impact Is the promised outcome backed by evidence and logic?
Scaling Reliability Can the system grow without catastrophic failure?
System Fit Does the design address the specific problem machine described?

If an evaluation does not explicitly touch these four questions, it is not an ACAD decision.

A small but powerful discipline

Evaluators are trained to write ballots using this sentence:

“I would fund / adopt Team ___’s system because…”

This forces evaluators to think like decision makers.

Not spectators.

How Evaluators interact during the round

Evaluators may:

  • ask clarification questions after the round (if the tournament allows it),

  • request to see Action Boards more clearly,

  • refer directly to components of the boards during deliberation.

Evaluators may not:

  • intervene during speeches,

  • coach teams,

  • suggest fixes,

  • reward entertaining delivery.

A warning to debaters

If you try to win an ACAD round by:

  • over-framing,

  • rhetorical traps,

  • fast technical overwhelm,

  • emotional appeals,

you are not playing the game the Evaluators are evaluating.

You are playing the wrong game.

The quiet power of the Evaluator role

By changing “judge” to “Evaluator”, ACAD changes something deep:

It teaches students that real influence is not earned by speaking better.

It is earned by building better.

That is the culture ACAD protects.

PART IV – Debate Format & Timing

8. The ACAD Debate Structure

How an ACAD round actually unfolds — and why the order is designed the way it is

An ACAD round is not built like a speech contest.

It is built like a real project pitch and technical review process.

You do not rush to sell your idea. You first explain the system. Then you design. Then you prove you can deliver. Then you prove it matters.

Only after all of that… you are allowed to fight.

This structure is intentional.

It trains students to think the way real solution teams work.

Before we show the table, let us first understand one very important idea:

ACAD is a build → test → compare → evaluate cycle.

Every role appears exactly when the evaluator needs that information.

8.1 Official ACAD round structure

Each team has four speakers, each with a fixed professional role.

The speaking order is identical for both teams.

There is no side advantage.

Official order and timing

Order Speaker Role Time
1 System Lead 4 minutes
2 Design Lead 5 minutes
3 Delivery Lead 5 minutes
→ Cross-Examination Block 4 minutes per team
4 Impact Lead 3 minutes

After both teams finish their Impact Lead speeches, there is an additional stage:

Evaluator Cross-Examination – unlimited time

(we will explain exactly what that means below)

This timing is written as:

4 – 5 – 5 – 4 – 3

This is the official ACAD format.

8.2 Why this order exists

ACAD does not allow you to start with your solution.

You must earn the right to design.

The logic is simple:

You cannot design before you understand. You cannot deliver before you design. You cannot measure before you deliver.

That is why the order is:

System → Design → Delivery → Impact

If you reverse this order, you would be debating.

In ACAD, you are building.

8.3 Speaker 1 – System Lead (4 minutes)

This is where the round truly begins.

Not with a hook. Not with a story. Not with a moral claim.

With a diagnosis.

In these four minutes, the System Lead must:

  • show how the current system works

  • identify the real bottlenecks

  • show where decisions are made

  • reveal why the problem keeps repeating

This speech answers only one big question:

What machine is producing this failure?

If the System Lead rushes, simplifies or skips complexity, the entire team becomes fragile.

Everything that follows depends on this map.

This is why ACAD gives only four minutes here:

Not because the role is less important — but because clarity, not volume, is rewarded.

8.4 Speaker 2 – Design Lead (5 minutes)

Once the system is visible, the Design Lead enters.

This is where ACAD becomes different from almost every other debate format.

The Design Lead must:

  • select leverage points

  • redesign processes

  • change relationships between actors

  • show what changes inside the system

This is not a list of programs.

It is a redesign of behaviour.

The Design Lead answers:

What exactly do we change so the system behaves differently?

These five minutes are the architectural heart of the round.

Evaluators are watching very carefully here:

Does this design really touch the bottleneck identified earlier?

Or is it simply a nice project sitting beside the real problem?

8.5 Speaker 3 – Delivery Lead (5 minutes)

Now comes the most dangerous moment in ACAD.

Because this is where beautiful designs usually collapse.

The Delivery Lead must now show:

  • who runs the system

  • who pays

  • who coordinates

  • who monitors

  • who resolves conflict

  • how operations unfold over time

This is not administration.

This is survival planning.

The Delivery Lead answers:

If this started tomorrow, who would actually do what?

Evaluators listen here like implementation officers.

Not like debate judges.

They are looking for:

  • hidden dependencies

  • unrealistic capacity assumptions

  • missing institutions

  • coordination overload

If delivery fails, impact is imaginary.

8.6 The Cross-Examination Block

4 minutes per team

This is where ACAD becomes truly competitive.

But not in the usual debating way.

After both teams have presented:

  • their systems,

  • their designs,

  • and their delivery models,

the round moves into a structured testing phase.

Each team receives:

4 minutes of cross-examination time.

This time is owned by the team.

Not by individual speakers.

What happens during this block?

You do not attack rhetoric.

You do not trap wording.

You test systems.

Teams should use this block to:

  • stress-test delivery chains

  • expose incentive failures

  • challenge scaling assumptions

  • question governance and coordination

  • probe financial sustainability

  • test actor cooperation

A good ACAD cross-examination sounds like:

“Who approves that transition?”

“Which institution owns that function after year one?”

“What happens when this partner withdraws?”

“How does this scale beyond pilot districts?”

A weak ACAD cross-examination sounds like:

“Isn’t your plan too ambitious?”

That is not testing.

That is opinion.

Important rule

Any member of the team may ask or answer questions.

The team operates as one design unit.

8.7 Speaker 4 – Impact Lead (3 minutes)

After design and delivery have been tested, the final speaker closes the system.

The Impact Lead does not summarise.

They evaluate.

The Impact Lead must:

  • define success metrics

  • explain how impact is measured

  • show attribution logic

  • explain learning and correction loops

  • present scaling pathways

  • compare performance with the other team

This speech answers:

How do we know this worked — and why is this system stronger than the alternative?

The three minutes are short on purpose.

This forces discipline.

No storytelling.

Only results.

8.8 The Evaluator Cross-Examination

(infinite timing)

This is one of the most powerful and unique features of ACAD.

After both teams finish their Impact Lead speeches, the floor opens to the Evaluators.

There is no fixed time limit.

This stage exists because:

real decision-makers are allowed to ask until they understand.

What happens here?

Evaluators may:

  • ask clarification questions

  • request deeper explanation of system links

  • probe delivery risks

  • challenge assumptions

  • ask about scale constraints

  • ask about monitoring and failure recovery

Evaluators do not debate with teams.

They investigate them.

This phase continues until:

The Evaluators believe they have enough information to decide.

This is why the timing is written as:

Evaluator cross-examination – unlimited

It reinforces a deep cultural message:

In the real world, your project is not finished when you stop talking. It is finished when decision-makers understand it.

8.9 A critical cultural rule

During Evaluator questioning:

Teams are not allowed to introduce entirely new systems or new interventions.

You may:

  • clarify,

  • specify,

  • deepen,

  • justify.

You may not redesign.

Your design must already exist.

This protects fairness and design integrity.

8.10 Why ACAD has no rebuttal speeches

Traditional debate ends with rebuttals.

ACAD ends with evaluation.

Why?

Because ACAD does not care who responded more cleverly.

It cares which system survives deeper scrutiny.

Cross-examination replaces rebuttal.

Evaluator questioning replaces judge deliberation theatre.

Impact comparison replaces narrative framing.

8.11 How to mentally prepare for an ACAD round

You should not prepare like a speaker.

You should prepare like a team facing a review board.

Ask yourselves:

  • Where would this system fail?

  • Which assumptions are fragile?

  • Which actor could block us?

  • Which budget line could collapse us?

  • Which coordination link is weakest?

If you can answer those questions before the round begins,

you will survive this structure.

If you cannot,

the structure will expose you.

That is exactly why ACAD uses this format.

9. The ACAD Cross-Examination

Where ideas stop being safe — and systems start being tested

If speeches in ACAD are where you build, cross-examination is where you prove the building can stand.

This is not a courtroom. It is not a political showdown. It is a design review.

In ACAD, cross-examination exists for one purpose only:

to stress-test whether a proposed system can survive reality.

9.1 The real purpose of cross-examination in ACAD

In traditional debate, cross-examination is used to:

  • trap wording,

  • force concessions,

  • destabilise speakers,

  • gain rhetorical advantage.

In ACAD, none of those are the goal.

The goal is much simpler — and much harder:

to find where a system will break.

Think of ACAD cross-examination as what happens when a project team presents to a review panel and the panel starts asking:

  • “Have you thought about…?”

  • “What happens if…?”

  • “Who actually decides…?”

  • “How do you handle failure…?”

This is the mindset you must bring.

A perfect ACAD cross-examination is not aggressive.

It is surgical.

9.2 What an ACAD cross-examination is allowed to question

Everything you ask must belong to one of the six approved testing zones.

If your question does not fall into one of these zones, it does not belong in ACAD.

1. System logic

You are allowed to question:

  • whether the system map is complete,

  • whether the identified bottlenecks are real,

  • whether the leverage points actually influence the outcome.

Examples:

“Which actor actually controls that transition?”

“Why is this the real bottleneck rather than the licensing gate?”

“What part of the system remains unchanged after your intervention?”

This is not disagreement.

It is verification.

2. Assumptions

You are allowed to challenge assumptions that the system depends on.

Examples:

“You assume hotels are willing to host trainees. What incentive makes that rational for them?”

“You assume youth can relocate. What covers their transition costs?”

“You assume data collection is possible. Which institution currently has that capacity?”

ACAD requires hidden assumptions to be surfaced.

3. Feasibility

You are allowed to question:

  • institutional capacity,

  • administrative complexity,

  • legal or regulatory barriers,

  • coordination requirements.

Examples:

“Which ministry legally owns that function today?”

“Which existing agency has staff to manage this?”

“What legal authority allows this data sharing?”

Feasibility is not about whether something is good.

It is about whether it can be done.

4. Scaling pathway

You are allowed to test:

  • how the model grows,

  • how replication happens,

  • what changes when the system moves from pilot to national scale.

Examples:

“Does your cost per participant decrease or increase at scale?”

“Which components must be standardised for replication?”

“What fails first when you double the number of sites?”

ACAD treats scaling as a design problem, not a future hope.

5. Cost and operations

You are allowed to question:

  • budget logic,

  • funding stability,

  • operational sequencing,

  • workload distribution.

Examples:

“How much does this cost per participant per year?”

“Which institution absorbs overhead?”

“What happens when donor funding ends?”

“Who carries financial risk if targets are missed?”

A system without financial and operational clarity is not a system.

It is an idea.

6. Delivery risk and failure management

You are allowed to question:

  • contingency planning,

  • monitoring mechanisms,

  • corrective feedback loops.

Examples:

“How do you detect early failure?”

“Who has authority to redesign components mid-implementation?”

“What happens if a partner withdraws?”

ACAD assumes failure will happen.

Strong systems are designed to recover.

9.3 What is not allowed in ACAD cross-examination

ACAD formally forbids three things.

1. Rhetorical traps

You may not:

  • ask loaded questions,

  • force false dichotomies,

  • misframe the other team’s model to score points.

Examples of what is not allowed:

“So you agree your system cannot work in rural areas?”

This is not testing.

This is manipulation.

2. Personal attacks

You may not:

  • attack a speaker’s competence,

  • question motives,

  • mock delivery,

  • imply ignorance.

ACAD is a professional space.

You attack systems.

Not people.

3. Misrepresentation

You may not:

  • oversimplify the other team’s design,

  • ignore stated safeguards,

  • collapse multiple components into one to weaken it.

If you do not understand a component, you must ask for clarification — not exploit confusion.

ACAD punishes strategic misunderstanding.

9.4 How to ask a good ACAD question

A strong ACAD question always contains:

  • a reference to a specific component of the other team’s Action Board

  • a clear operational concern

  • a request for mechanism, not opinion

For example:

“In your delivery chain, after district offices approve placements, who verifies employer capacity before onboarding youth?”

That question:

  • points to a real system step,

  • targets a risk,

  • asks for an operational answer.

A weak ACAD question sounds like:

“Don’t you think your model is unrealistic?”

That tests nothing.

9.5 How to answer in ACAD cross-examination

This is extremely important.

You are not allowed to redesign your solution.

But you are expected to:

  • clarify how existing components work,

  • specify responsibilities,

  • explain sequences,

  • show safeguards.

If your system cannot answer a reasonable question without inventing new institutions or programs, the design is fragile.

ACAD treats cross-examination as:

a visibility tool.

Not a repair tool.

9.6 A practical discipline for teams

Before every round, ACAD teams should perform one exercise:

Each role lead asks:

“What would I attack in our own system?”

If you cannot attack your own design,

you have not thought deeply enough.

9.7 Why ACAD cross-examination replaces rebuttals

ACAD does not allow rebuttal speeches because:

rebuttals train narrative defence.

Cross-examination trains system defence.

In ACAD, you do not win by sounding resilient.

You win by being resilient.

9.8 The cultural line that protects ACAD

Remember this line.

If your question would not be asked in a real project review meeting, it does not belong in ACAD.

This single rule protects the format from becoming another argumentative debate.

It keeps ACAD what it is meant to be:

a serious competition of real solutions.

PART V – The Action Board (Core of ACAD)

10. What is the Action Board?

The beating heart of ACAD — where ideas become visible, testable, and comparable

If ACAD were a human body, the Action Board would be the skeleton. Everything else — speeches, cross-examination, and discussion — hangs on it.

This is not a decoration. It is not a visual aid like a Power Point slide to support a narrative. It is a working model of your solution, visible to both your team and the Evaluators.

Without it, your speeches are invisible. Without it, your system is imaginary. Without it, ACAD is impossible.

10.1 Why ACAD requires visual/system presentation

In traditional debate, ideas are carried on words. You speak. You argue. Judges remember.

In ACAD, ideas are carried on systems. You design. You show. Evaluators verify.

The Action Board exists for three fundamental reasons:

  • Clarity: Complex systems and interventions cannot live in words alone.

  • Comparability: Evaluators must be able to compare two solutions side by side, component by component.

  • Accountability: A solution exists only if it can be inspected, tested, and traced.

Think of it as the blueprint you give a city council before they fund a project. If it’s unclear, the money doesn’t flow. If it’s incomplete, the project fails.

10.2 Action Board vs Slides

Many teams make the mistake of treating an Action Board like a presentation slide deck.

Slides are for storytelling. They are linear. They emphasize style over structure.

Action Boards are for system visibility. They are multi-layered. They emphasize logic, delivery, impact, and evaluation.

A proper Action Board shows:

  • System map → who does what, flows, actors, constraints

  • Design layer → interventions and leverage points

  • Delivery layer → operational steps, ownership, coordination

  • Impact layer → metrics, monitoring, scalability

Every component must be visible at a glance. Every connection must be traceable.

Visual vs Text

  • Text: Labels, actors, steps, metrics

  • Visuals: Arrows, diagrams, charts, icons showing flows and dependencies

  • Rule: Every element on the board must correspond to something explained in a speech

10.3 What Evaluators can use the Action Board for

Evaluators are not passive observers. They are investigators.

They use the Action Board to:

  • Verify logic: Does the design follow from the system?

  • Trace delivery chains: Who executes what? Where are dependencies?

  • Compare impact: Which team’s system produces measurable outcomes more effectively?

  • Stress-test feasibility: Where are the bottlenecks, weak links, or unrealistic assumptions?

  • Follow cross-examination questions: Refer to specific elements without relying on memory

If an evaluator cannot follow your system on the board, your arguments lose clarity. If they cannot trace impact, your solution loses credibility.

What the Action Board is NOT

  • Not a slide deck for dramatic reveals

  • Not a storytelling tool for rhetoric

  • Not a flashy decoration for style points

  • Not a list of programs without interconnections

10.4 The cultural rule of the Action Board

In ACAD, if it is not on the Action Board, it does not exist.

Every system, intervention, delivery mechanism, and metric must be visible on the board.

Speeches bring it to life. Cross-examination tests it. Evaluators rely on it to make final decisions.

The Action Board is your solution made real.

It is the bridge between ideas and impact.

Without it, you are speaking in the air. With it, you are building the future — in front of the world.

11. The Universal ACAD Action Board Template

Your blueprint for building, showing, and defending solutions in ACAD

The Universal ACAD Action Board Template is your team’s roadmap. It is standardized so that every solution can be evaluated fairly and compared rigorously.

Think of it as the “DNA” of every ACAD solution. If your Action Board follows this structure, you speak the language of the Evaluators. If it doesn’t, your system risks being invisible.

Every team must design their solution using this exact logic. The sequence is intentional — it mirrors real-world project planning and implementation.

Section 1 – Problem System Map

This is the foundation. Before you build a solution, you must show the system.

Include:

  • System actors: All stakeholders, institutions, and influencers involved.

  • Bottlenecks: Points where the system fails or slows down.

  • Failures: Patterns causing recurring problems.

  • Feedback loops: How actions create self-reinforcing or corrective cycles.

Example: In Rwanda’s tourism value chain:

  • Actors: Ministry of Tourism, hotels, youth associations, tour operators, local communities.

  • Bottlenecks: Limited youth training, low financing, poor coordination between actors.

  • Failures: Youth leave sector, tourism opportunities unexploited.

  • Feedback loops: Youth leave → less innovation → fewer tourists → less revenue → fewer youth opportunities.

Section 2 – Intervention Architecture

Here is where your design comes to life.

Include:

  • Components of the solution: Programs, processes, tools, or policies.

  • How they interact: The logic and connections between components.

  • Who controls what: Responsibilities and authority.

Example:

  • Component A: Youth training hub.

  • Component B: Internship placements in hotels.

  • Component C: Digital platform for tourism project tracking.

  • Interaction: Hub feeds trained youth to internships → platform tracks progress → Ministry reports metrics.

  • Control: Hub led by youth associations, internships managed by hotels, Ministry oversees platform.

Section 3 – Delivery Pathway

Show step-by-step implementation logic.

Include:

  • Step-by-step implementation: From launch to full operation.

  • Ownership and responsibilities: Which actor does what at every stage.

  • Operational workflow: Sequence of actions, approvals, and coordination.

Example:

  • Recruit youth → 2. Train at hub → 3. Place in hotels → 4. Monitor progress → 5. Report outcomes → 6. Adjust programs.

Section 4 – Resource & Constraint Model

Identify what your system needs and what might limit it.

Include:

  • Skills: Staff, technical knowledge, training.

  • Money: Budget, funding sources.

  • Infrastructure: Facilities, equipment, technology.

  • Legal/regulatory issues: Compliance, approvals, licenses.

  • Social barriers: Cultural resistance, awareness gaps.

Example:

  • Skills: 20 trainers, 5 coordinators

  • Money: $50k startup, government co-funding

  • Infrastructure: Training hub in Kigali

  • Legal: Ministry approval for internships

  • Social: Youth willingness, community acceptance

Section 5 – Impact Logic Chain

Demonstrate how your actions translate to real impact.

Chain Element Description Example (Tourism Sector)
Activities 6-month training programs Conducting structured youth training modules.
Outputs 100 youth trained, 80 placed Quantifiable direct results of activities.
Outcomes Increased employment Changes in behavior or institutional performance.
Impact Strengthened Sector Long-term sectoral or national improvements.

Section 6 – Measurement Framework

Explain how you will prove your system works.

Framework Component Goal Example
Indicators Success metrics Retention rates, placement numbers, innovation scores.
Data Sources Verification origin Hotel payrolls, hub registries, Ministry reports.
Monitoring Method Collection logic Quarterly audits by district tourism offices.

Example:

  • Indicator: % of youth employed in tourism within 3 months

  • Data source: Internship platform reports, hotel HR data

  • Monitoring method: Quarterly dashboard, Ministry verification

Section 7 – Scaling & Replication Strategy

Show how your system grows beyond the pilot.

Scaling Dimension Growth Strategy Example (2-Year Horizon)
Geographic Expansion to districts Start Kigali → scale to 5 districts.
Population Target demographic 100 youth/district → 500+ total at scale.
Institutional Model replication Youth associations adopt; Ministry standardizes.

Section 8 – Risk & Failure Modes

Be honest about what could go wrong. Evaluators love clarity here.

Identified Risk Mitigation Strategy Failure Management
Hotel placement refusal Contractual incentives Legal agreements + tax/benefit offsets.
Funding shortage Multi-stream resource Community partnerships + donor blends.
Low youth retention Stipends & Awareness Direct engagement + socioeconomic support.

The Core Principle of the Universal Action Board

If it is not on the Action Board, it does not exist.

Every component, actor, metric, and risk must be visible. The Action Board is your system made tangible.

It allows your team to:

  • Speak clearly

  • Show proof of design

  • Defend against cross-examination

  • Persuade Evaluators with evidence, not rhetoric

If you follow this template, your team is ready for any ACAD challenge — and every Evaluator will be able to see, test, and compare your system without confusion.

PART VI – How Speakers Use the Action Board

In ACAD, speakers do not “cover arguments”. They operate a system in public.

The Action Board is not a visual aid. It is the arena where your solution lives — and where it can be broken.

Each speaker is responsible for one layer of the same living structure.

If one layer collapses, the whole system collapses.

This chapter teaches you how each role must use the Action Board — not talk around it.

12. System Lead

You do not introduce the topic. You introduce the system that traps the problem.

The System Lead opens the debate by answering one brutal question:

“What exactly is broken — and how is it currently supposed to work?”

Not:

  • how sad the situation is

  • how urgent it feels

  • how unfair the problem looks

But:

How the machine actually runs today.

Your only real tool is Section 1 – Problem System Map.

1. Your first duty: make the invisible system visible

Most debates begin like this:

“Youth are excluded because of lack of opportunities.”

ACAD forbids that sentence.

Instead, you show:

  • who controls entry into the value chain

  • who decides access to training

  • who hires

  • who certifies

  • who pays

  • who coordinates

  • who benefits

In the Rwanda tourism example, a strong System Lead would not say:

“Youth lack skills.”

They would say:

“The tourism labor pipeline is controlled by three institutional gates: training institutions, private operators, and licensing bodies — and youth are not structurally connected to any of them.”

That is a system claim.

2. You must show flows, not categories

Your board must show:

  • how information flows

  • how money flows

  • how people flow

  • how decisions flow

A weak map lists actors.

A strong map shows movement.

For example:

Youth → training provider → certificate → employer → salary But in reality:

Youth → informal learning → no certification → no employer access → exit sector

That broken pathway is the debate.

3. Your second duty: explain why the current system fails by design

Do not say:

“The government is not doing enough.”

Say:

“The system was designed to optimize compliance and quality control, not inclusion and workforce expansion.”

This is a critical ACAD skill:

You never accuse people. You diagnose design.

4. You must identify at least one structural feedback loop

Evaluators will look for this.

Example:

Low youth entry → fewer young entrepreneurs → less innovation → weaker tourism experiences → lower demand → tighter hiring → even lower youth entry.

If you fail to show a loop, your system is shallow.

5. Your final duty: frame the design challenge

Your last 30 seconds must answer:

“If we were allowed to redesign this system, what must change structurally?”

Not:

“We should train more youth.”

But:

“We must redesign the way youth are inserted into the labor and enterprise pipeline of tourism.”

Your job ends by handing the problem to the Design Lead as a design challenge, not as a moral appeal.

The System Lead does not sell solutions. They create the necessity for redesign.

13. Design Lead

You do not propose activities. You architect a system.

Your home is:

Section 2 – Intervention Architecture

1. Your first rule: never describe components in isolation

Bad design sounds like this:

“We create a training program. We create a mentorship program. We create a platform.”

That is a list.

ACAD wants an architecture.

You must show:

  • which component triggers which

  • which component depends on which

  • which component controls which decision

If you remove one component, the whole system must visibly weaken.

2. You must show the control logic

Every real system has power.

Who approves? Who selects? Who allocates? Who enforces?

If your system has no authority logic, it is fictional.

For example:

  • who selects youth into the program?

  • who certifies completion?

  • who can block entry?

  • who can remove partners?

Design Leads must mark control clearly.

3. You must explicitly link every component to a failure in the old system

Take one failure from the System Lead map and show:

“This component exists only because this failure exists.”

If you cannot trace that link, your design is ornamental.

4. You must show interaction, not explanation

A powerful moment in ACAD is when you physically walk the board:

“Here is the youth intake module. It feeds into certification. Certification unlocks employer onboarding. Employer onboarding triggers wage subsidy activation.”

That walk is more persuasive than any rhetoric.

5. You must defend design logic, not defend morality

Cross-examination will attack you like this:

“Why is this better than simply funding existing institutions?”

Your answer must be:

  • structural

  • architectural

  • dependency-based

Not value-based.

Design Leads build systems that can be interrogated. If it cannot be interrogated, it is not a design.

14. Delivery Lead

You are the moment where most debate models collapse. ACAD exists because of you.

Your board is:

  • Section 3 – Delivery Pathway

  • Section 4 – Resource & Constraint Model

1. You translate architecture into reality

Design says: “This should work.”

Delivery says: “This can be executed by real actors under real constraints.”

2. You must show a real operational timeline

Not phases like: “short-term, mid-term, long-term”.

But:

  • what happens first

  • who initiates

  • who approves

  • who implements

  • what enables the next step

If someone asked:

“Who wakes up on day one and starts this?”

You must be able to point to them.

3. You must show institutional embedding

ACAD forbids floating pilots.

Your system must land inside:

  • ministries

  • companies

  • schools

  • cooperatives

  • NGOs

  • local governments

You must show:

Which institution absorbs which function.

If your system disappears when donor funding stops, it is not an ACAD system.

4. You must face constraints openly

Strong Delivery Leads are honest.

You must name:

  • skill shortages

  • regulatory delays

  • procurement bottlenecks

  • coordination costs

  • social resistance

Then show:

what operational mechanism absorbs that risk.

Not hope. Not “awareness”.

Mechanism.

5. You must prove feasibility, not claim it

Feasibility is shown by:

  • realistic staffing loads

  • plausible budgets

  • institutional capacity

  • legal pathways

If your design requires a ministry to suddenly perform like a startup, Evaluators will reject it.

Delivery Leads protect ACAD from fantasy.

15. Impact Lead

You do not summarise the debate. You compare systems.

Your board is:

  • Section 5 – Impact Logic Chain

  • Section 6 – Measurement Framework

  • Section 7 – Scaling Strategy

1. Your first duty: compare impact models — not outcomes

Do not say:

“We create more jobs than them.”

Say:

“Our system changes the entry structure of the tourism workforce; theirs improves preparation without restructuring access.”

You compare:

  • what layer of the system changes

  • how deep the change penetrates

  • how persistent the change is

2. You must walk the logic chain slowly

Activities → outputs → outcomes → impact

If there is a jump, you must justify it.

Example:

Training → employment

That jump is illegal unless you show:

certification → employer access → hiring commitment → placement mechanism

Your job is to guard causal credibility.

3. You must compare measurement credibility

Not: “we will monitor.”

But:

  • what indicators are actually measurable

  • who controls the data

  • how manipulation is prevented

  • how often feedback updates the system

ACAD treats monitoring as part of the system — not an evaluation add-on.

4. You must compare scalability pathways — not scale numbers

Do not say:

“We can reach 100,000 youth.”

Say:

“Our system scales by institutional replication; theirs scales by operational expansion.”

Then compare:

  • which is politically easier

  • which is financially lighter

  • which is administratively realistic

5. You must crystallise superiority at system level

Your final framing must sound like this:

“This debate is not about two projects. It is about two system redesigns. One changes the access structure. The other optimises delivery inside an unchanged structure.”

That is ACAD crystallisation.

6. You never appeal to sympathy

You appeal to:

  • structural leverage

  • causal certainty

  • institutional durability

  • scaling logic

Impact Leads end the debate by making one system clearly harder to replace, harder to reverse, and easier to grow.

A final truth about Part VI

In ACAD:

  • System Leads diagnose.

  • Design Leads architect.

  • Delivery Leads operationalise.

  • Impact Leads compare futures.

Together, you do not defend a side.

You present a functioning alternative reality — and invite the Evaluators to test whether it could truly exist.

PART VII – Comparative Logic (The Heart of ACAD)

Before ACAD, most debate formats trained students to win arguments.

ACAD trains students to choose between futures.

This part of the manual is the most important chapter you will ever read.

If you misunderstand comparison, you will still sound brilliant — and still lose.

Because in ACAD:

The winner is not the team with the best speeches. The winner is the team whose system survives comparison.

16. What Comparison Means in ACAD

Let’s kill a very dangerous habit first.

In traditional debate, comparison sounds like this:

“Our plan helps more people.” “Their plan is unrealistic.” “Our impacts are bigger.”

In ACAD, those sentences are meaningless unless they answer one deeper question:

Which system would actually perform better if both were deployed in the real world?

Not on paper. Not in a judge’s imagination. In reality.

Comparison in ACAD is not outcome vs outcome.

It is:

system vs system.

Think about what you are actually putting into the room.

Each team brings:

  • a system map

  • an intervention architecture

  • a delivery pathway

  • a resource model

  • an impact chain

  • a scaling logic

  • a risk profile

Those are the objects being compared.

Not your talking points.

The biggest mistake new ACAD debaters make

They wait until the Impact Lead to start comparing.

That is too late.

In ACAD:

  • System Leads compare problem framings.

  • Design Leads compare architectures.

  • Delivery Leads compare execution models.

  • Impact Leads only crystallise what has already been compared.

If comparison appears only in the last three minutes, your system has already lost.

A short story (why ACAD was built this way)

In an early pilot of ACAD, two student teams worked on a youth employment challenge.

One team presented a very impressive training programme. The other team redesigned the recruitment and contracting pipeline between companies and training institutions.

When judges discussed afterwards, someone said:

“Both teams had good ideas. It was hard to choose.”

That is exactly what ACAD is designed to prevent.

Why?

Because in the real world, policymakers do not choose “good ideas”.

They choose which system to build.

So ACAD forces you to compare systems under stress.

The six compulsory comparison dimensions

Every ACAD round must compare the two systems on all of the following.

Not as a checklist.

As living trade-offs.

1. System robustness

This asks:

How many parts must work perfectly for this system to succeed?

A fragile system looks elegant on a board.

A robust system still functions when:

  • one partner drops out

  • one institution delays

  • funding arrives late

  • political attention shifts

You must show:

  • where redundancy exists

  • where single points of failure exist

In comparison:

“Their model depends on one central coordinating unit. Ours distributes coordination across existing institutions.”

That is a robustness comparison.

2. Delivery feasibility

This asks:

Who actually carries the operational burden?

Not: “Is it theoretically possible?”

But:

Can the organisations named realistically absorb this work?

You compare:

  • institutional capacity

  • operational complexity

  • legal and procurement pathways

  • coordination costs

A system that looks smaller but is easier to execute often beats a more ambitious one.

3. Speed to impact

This asks:

When does real change start happening?

Not when the pilot starts.

Not when the proposal is approved.

When:

  • youth actually enter new jobs

  • firms actually change behaviour

  • access actually shifts

You must compare the activation points in your delivery pathway.

4. Depth of impact

This asks:

What layer of the system is being changed?

Surface change:

  • skills

  • awareness

  • short-term opportunities

Structural change:

  • access rules

  • contracting structures

  • certification and recognition systems

  • market linkages

Depth is not emotional.

It is architectural.

5. Scalability

This asks:

How does the system grow?

Not: “How many people can it reach?”

But:

By what mechanism does it expand?

You compare:

  • operational scaling (more staff, more funding, more centres) vs

  • institutional replication (copying the model into new institutions, regions, or sectors)

One is heavy. The other is lighter.

ACAD prefers scalable mechanisms — not scalable budgets.

6. Resilience to failure

This asks:

What happens when something goes wrong?

You must compare:

  • failure containment

  • feedback loops

  • learning mechanisms

  • correction pathways

A system that anticipates failure beats a system that pretends failure will not happen.

What ACAD comparison is NOT

ACAD comparison is not:

  • who spoke better

  • who sounded more confident

  • who had more statistics

  • who had more emotional stories

If an Evaluator says:

“Team A was more persuasive”

and cannot explain which system performed better under comparison,

the round has been judged incorrectly.

17. The ACAD Weighing Framework

Once you understand what must be compared, ACAD gives you an official way to weigh those comparisons.

These are not optional.

They are the shared language of decision-making in ACAD.

They are called:

the official weighing lenses.

Lens 1 — Reach

Reach answers:

Who is actually touched by this system?

But in ACAD, reach is not only population size.

You must specify:

  • which segment of youth

  • which actors in the value chain

  • which geographic or institutional zones

You compare:

“Their system reaches urban hospitality students. Ours reaches informal youth operators in rural tourism clusters.”

That is reach.

Lens 2 — Depth

Depth answers:

What actually changes in someone’s position in the system?

Depth is not: “How many workshops?”

Depth is:

  • change in access rights

  • change in bargaining power

  • change in income stability

  • change in market position

In ACAD, depth always ties back to the system map.

If you cannot point to a node or a flow that changes, depth is zero.

Lens 3 — Sustainability

Sustainability answers:

Can the system survive after initial support fades?

This includes:

  • financial sustainability

  • institutional ownership

  • political insulation

  • operational continuity

In comparison, you must show:

“Their system collapses without external coordination funding. Ours is embedded inside routine institutional budgets.”

That is sustainability.

Lens 4 — Replicability

Replicability answers:

Can another region, district, institution or sector copy this system?

You compare:

  • how many unique conditions are required

  • how specialised the skills are

  • how dependent the model is on personalities or champions

A system that only works where its designers exist is not an ACAD-quality system.

Lens 5 — Institutional alignment

This lens is uniquely important in ACAD.

It asks:

Does this system fit how institutions actually behave?

Not how you wish they behaved.

But how they are structured to behave.

You must compare:

  • incentive alignment

  • reporting structures

  • authority boundaries

  • bureaucratic logic

Many beautiful student systems lose here.

Because they require institutions to act against their own incentives.

ACAD punishes that.

How to actually weigh (not list) lenses

A very common failure looks like this:

“On reach, we win. On depth, we win. On sustainability, we win.”

That is not weighing.

That is labelling.

Real ACAD weighing sounds like this:

“Even if their system reaches more youth in the first year, our system permanently restructures entry pathways. So we lose on short-term reach but win on depth and sustainability — and those two lenses dominate because this debate is about fixing a broken value chain, not running an employment drive.”

Weighing is about trade-offs.

Not tallies.

A powerful mental model

Imagine both systems are deployed tomorrow.

Not together.

Separately.

In two similar countries.

Five years later, an independent evaluation team walks into both systems.

Your job is to convince the Evaluators that:

the world produced by your system is structurally better.

Not happier.

Not louder.

Better designed.

The most important sentence in this entire chapter

In ACAD:

You do not win by proving your system is good. You win by proving the other system is weaker in the exact same reality.

A final warning to new ACAD debaters

If you leave the round thinking:

“We presented a strong solution.”

You may still lose.

If you leave the round thinking:

“We showed why their system breaks earlier, scales slower, and embeds less deeply than ours.”

You are finally debating in ACAD.

PART VIII – Evidence & Research

18. Evidence in ACAD

There is a quiet revolution hidden inside ACAD.

It is not about how you speak. It is not about how your Action Board looks.

It is about what kind of truth you bring into the room.

In most debates, evidence is something you quote.

In ACAD, evidence is something you build with.

If your system is the machine, your evidence is the material it is made from.

Weak material → beautiful machine → collapse.

Before we define types of evidence, you must internalise one rule:

In ACAD, evidence exists to justify design decisions, not to decorate speeches.

If you use evidence only to say “the problem is big”, you are doing old debate.

The biggest mistake debaters make with evidence

They collect facts about the problem.

ACAD needs facts about:

  • how organisations behave,

  • how markets move,

  • how people actually respond to incentives,

  • how systems fail during implementation.

ACAD evidence is operational.

Let us walk slowly through the four evidence families used in ACAD.

Not as categories.

As tools.

1. Academic evidence

Academic evidence answers:

How does this system behave in theory and under controlled analysis?

This includes:

  • peer-reviewed studies,

  • formal evaluations,

  • policy research,

  • economic and social science models.

Academic evidence is strongest for:

  • understanding mechanisms,

  • identifying causal relationships,

  • validating assumptions about behaviour.

For example:

If your design assumes that vocational certification increases employability, academic studies can justify:

  • whether certification matters,

  • under what labour market conditions it matters,

  • and when it does not.

But here is the danger

Academic evidence usually lives far away from:

  • your institutions,

  • your legal environment,

  • your political economy,

  • your delivery constraints.

So in ACAD:

Academic evidence is never sufficient on its own.

It only tells you what could work.

Not what will work here.

2. Industry reports

Industry evidence answers:

How does the sector actually operate right now?

This includes:

  • market studies,

  • sector reviews,

  • employer surveys,

  • business and investment reports,

  • professional association data.

Industry reports are extremely powerful in ACAD because they reveal:

  • hiring practices,

  • skill gaps as perceived by employers,

  • operational bottlenecks,

  • commercial incentives.

In a tourism debate, for example, industry evidence tells you:

  • where demand is growing,

  • which roles are hard to fill,

  • which services are underdeveloped,

  • what operators complain about most.

But industry evidence also has bias

It often reflects:

  • large firms more than small ones,

  • formal operators more than informal actors,

  • current market structures more than future possibilities.

So ACAD uses industry evidence to understand:

how the system currently rewards and punishes behaviour.

Not to justify what the system should become.

3. Operational data

This is the most important type of evidence in ACAD.

Operational evidence answers:

What actually happens when people try to run similar systems?

This includes:

  • programme implementation records,

  • internal dashboards,

  • delivery statistics,

  • administrative data,

  • project management reports.

This is the data that tells you:

  • how long processes really take,

  • where approvals stall,

  • how many participants drop out,

  • how many partners fail to deliver.

In ACAD, operational data is gold.

Why?

Because it exposes friction.

An example of why this matters

A student team once proposed a youth tourism incubation pipeline.

Academically, incubation models looked effective.

Industry reports praised entrepreneurship.

But operational data from similar youth programmes showed:

  • extremely high early-stage dropout,

  • low conversion to sustainable enterprises,

  • high mentoring fatigue among volunteers.

The system did not fail because the idea was bad.

It failed because the delivery pipeline was overloaded.

Only operational evidence reveals that.

If you want to win ACAD rounds consistently:

Start hunting operational data early.

4. Pilot data

Pilot evidence answers:

What happens when this specific idea is tested in a limited form?

This includes:

  • small-scale trials,

  • local experiments,

  • organisational pilots,

  • proof-of-concept deployments.

Pilot data is powerful because it links:

design → delivery → outcome.

But it must be treated carefully.

ACAD rule on pilot evidence

Pilot data proves:

  • feasibility under controlled support,

  • not scalability under normal conditions.

So when you use pilot evidence, you must always ask:

  • Who supported the pilot?

  • What extra resources existed?

  • What shortcuts were allowed?

If your scaling model depends on pilot-level intensity, you must openly admit that.

ACAD punishes hidden assumptions.

Contextual validity – the invisible filter

Now comes the most important concept in this chapter.

Contextual validity.

It asks one brutal question:

Does this evidence describe behaviour under conditions similar to ours?

Not:

Is it credible?

Not:

Is it published?

But:

Is the operating environment comparable?

Context includes:

  • institutional maturity,

  • public sector capacity,

  • labour market structure,

  • regulatory complexity,

  • infrastructure availability,

  • cultural norms,

  • political incentives.

A study from a high-income, high-capacity state can still be perfectly correct…

…and completely useless for your system design.

In ACAD, you must always annotate your evidence mentally like this:

“This tells us what works when institutions are strong.” “This tells us what happens when funding is stable.” “This tells us what happens when private sector coordination is mature.”

Then ask:

Do those conditions exist here?

If they do not, you must adapt your system.

Not import the solution.

Avoiding imported solutions (the most dangerous shortcut)

One of the most seductive habits in student debates is this:

“In country X, they created Y programme, and it worked.”

ACAD is allergic to that sentence.

Not because learning is bad.

But because copying systems is intellectually lazy.

Why imported solutions fail in ACAD logic

Because systems are not just activities.

They are:

  • authority arrangements,

  • financing flows,

  • institutional incentives,

  • accountability structures.

When you import a solution without redesigning its system logic, you import assumptions.

And assumptions kill feasibility.

What ACAD allows instead

You may borrow:

  • design principles,

  • structural patterns,

  • governance models,

  • delivery mechanisms.

But you must always rebuild:

  • who controls,

  • who funds,

  • who enforces,

  • who absorbs operational risk.

Inside your own context.

A powerful ACAD sentence is:

“We borrow the contracting logic, not the institutional structure.”

That is mature research thinking.

How evidence is used differently by each speaker

This is critical.

System Lead

Uses evidence to justify:

  • actor relationships,

  • bottlenecks,

  • behavioural incentives,

  • systemic failures.

Not impact magnitude.

Design Lead

Uses evidence to justify:

  • component choices,

  • interaction logic,

  • governance and control arrangements.

Not programme popularity.

Delivery Lead

Uses evidence to justify:

  • timelines,

  • staffing loads,

  • institutional capacity,

  • budget plausibility,

  • regulatory feasibility.

This is where operational and pilot data dominate.

Impact Lead

Uses evidence to justify:

  • causal chains,

  • measurement credibility,

  • long-term effects,

  • scaling mechanisms.

This is where academic and evaluation evidence become most useful.

A quiet but powerful ACAD research habit

Before you write your Action Board, ask this for every major design choice:

What evidence made us choose this structure rather than another?

If the answer is:

  • intuition,

  • personal belief,

  • moral preference,

you are designing a project.

Not a system.

A final story

In one early ACAD round, a team built a beautiful youth tourism innovation hub.

They had:

  • strong academic justification,

  • glowing international case studies,

  • impressive pilot stories.

During cross-examination, the Delivery Lead of the opposing team asked only one question:

“Who approves land use and operational licensing for this hub?”

The team hesitated.

They had never checked.

Their entire delivery pathway rested on an invisible institutional gate.

They lost the round.

Not because their idea was weak.

But because their evidence had never touched the real system.

The core truth of evidence in ACAD

In ACAD:

Evidence does not prove that your idea is good.

It proves that your system can survive contact with reality.

And that is the only kind of truth that matters.

19. Research Workflow for ACAD Teams

In ACAD, research is not a homework task.

It is the engineering phase of your system.

If you research badly, you do not just lose arguments — you design the wrong machine.

This chapter shows you how an ACAD team actually builds knowledge, step by step, before touching the Action Board.

The ACAD research mindset

Before tools, before sources, before interviews, you must accept this rule:

ACAD research exists to reduce design risk.

Not to increase your bibliography.

Not to look impressive.

Every research activity must answer one of these questions:

  • Who controls this part of the system?

  • What actually blocks change?

  • What resources really exist?

  • What behaviour will resist our design?

Step 1 — Sector scanning

Sector scanning answers a very simple but often ignored question:

What does this sector really look like today?

Not how people describe it.

Not how strategy documents describe it.

But how it operates on the ground.

What sector scanning includes

You scan:

  • size of the sector

  • sub-segments and niches

  • formal vs informal activity

  • geographic concentration

  • growth trends

  • technological shifts

  • regulatory climate

In the Rwanda tourism example

A proper sector scan would distinguish:

  • hospitality vs experiences

  • tour operations vs transport

  • conservation vs community tourism

  • urban tourism vs rural tourism

  • domestic vs international demand

This matters because:

Each sub-segment has:

  • different entry barriers

  • different skills

  • different capital requirements

  • different licensing rules

ACAD forbids treating a sector as one block.

Output of sector scanning

You should be able to write one sentence:

“The dominant economic and institutional structure of this sector is X.”

If you cannot summarise the structure, you do not understand the system yet.

Step 2 — Actor interviews

This is the most underestimated research tool in student debates.

Actor interviews answer:

What do people inside the system actually experience?

Not what reports claim.

Who counts as an actor?

An actor is anyone who:

  • makes decisions,

  • controls access,

  • delivers services,

  • enforces rules,

  • or bears risk.

What ACAD interviews are not

They are not opinion polls.

They are not motivational conversations.

They are operational investigations.

What you must extract in interviews

You are looking for:

  • where delays occur

  • where approvals fail

  • where coordination breaks

  • where incentives misalign

  • where informal workarounds exist

A single good interview can reveal more than ten reports.

Example

If you interview a small tour operator and ask:

“What skills do you want from youth?”

You are doing old debate.

If you ask:

“What stops you from hiring or contracting young people right now?”

You are doing ACAD research.

Step 3 — Policy review

Policy review answers:

What is legally and institutionally possible?

Not what is recommended.

Not what is promised.

But what is authorised.

What ACAD policy review looks for

You review:

  • laws

  • regulations

  • ministerial directives

  • national strategies

  • operational guidelines

But you focus on:

  • mandates

  • powers

  • limitations

  • approval chains

The critical ACAD question

For every proposed system function, ask:

Which institution is legally allowed to do this?

If no institution has the mandate, your design must include:

either:

  • a policy change pathway

or:

  • a redesign of responsibilities

ACAD does not allow silent institutional invention.

Step 4 — Market mapping

Market mapping answers:

How does value actually move through the sector?

Money.

Services.

Information.

Contracts.

You map:

  • suppliers

  • intermediaries

  • buyers

  • service providers

  • regulators

  • financiers

And you trace:

  • who pays whom

  • for what

  • under what conditions

Why this matters

Many student systems try to “help youth” without touching the market.

But youth participation in a value chain is fundamentally a market problem.

If your system does not change how young people connect to buyers, clients, or employers, it is structurally weak.

Market mapping output

You should be able to draw:

  • entry points

  • choke points

  • monopoly positions

  • informal pathways

These directly feed into your Problem System Map.

Step 5 — Gap analysis

This is where ACAD becomes different from research projects.

Gap analysis answers:

Where exactly does the current system fail to deliver its intended function?

Not:

what is missing.

But:

what fails.

ACAD gap analysis compares three layers

  • What the system claims to do

  • What the system is designed to do

  • What the system actually produces

The gaps appear between these layers.

A powerful ACAD gap statement sounds like this:

“The tourism skills system produces certified graduates, but the labour market allocates jobs through informal networks, so certification is structurally disconnected from hiring.”

That is a design gap.

Not a capacity gap.

How this workflow feeds the Action Board

Your research workflow directly populates:

  • sector scanning → system context

  • actor interviews → bottlenecks & constraints

  • policy review → delivery feasibility & mandates

  • market mapping → flows & choke points

  • gap analysis → intervention logic

If you cannot point from a section of your Action Board to a research step, your design is speculative.

20. Referencing & Integrity

ACAD treats integrity as part of system quality.

If your evidence cannot be trusted, your system cannot be trusted.

Citation standards

ACAD does not require a specific academic referencing style.

It requires:

clarity.

Every piece of factual or operational evidence must be traceable to:

  • a document

  • a dataset

  • an interview

  • a public statement

  • or an internal record

You must be able to answer:

“Where did this information come from?”

In one sentence.

On the Action Board

Each section should indicate:

  • the source family (academic / industry / operational / pilot)

  • the organisation or author

  • the year

Not full references.

Traceability, not formatting, is the goal.

Transparency of assumptions

No system is built only from evidence.

Every system includes assumptions.

ACAD requires you to expose them.

Examples of assumptions you must declare

  • partner institutions will cooperate

  • funding will be approved

  • demand will remain stable

  • regulatory approval will be granted

  • technology adoption will occur

These must be marked explicitly in your design logic.

Hidden assumptions are design failures.

Not strategic advantages.

Ethical use of data

ACAD teams often work with:

  • youth data

  • community information

  • business-sensitive information

You must:

  • anonymise individuals

  • avoid publishing sensitive operational details

  • obtain consent for interviews where appropriate

ACAD will not reward teams that extract information irresponsibly.

No fabrication, no exaggeration, no selective reporting

This rule is strict.

You may not:

  • inflate numbers,

  • cherry-pick extreme cases,

  • hide negative findings.

If a pilot failed, that failure is evidence.

In ACAD, failure data strengthens design.

Integrity in interviews

If you conduct actor interviews, you must not:

  • misrepresent the purpose of the conversation,

  • pressure interviewees,

  • quote them out of context.

Summaries must reflect what was actually said.

A final principle

In ACAD, you are not performing research.

You are temporarily stepping into the role of a system designer.

That role carries responsibility.

A system built on distorted evidence may still win a debate.

But it would fail the world.

ACAD exists so that students do not practice designing failure.

PART IX – Attacking Solutions (ACAD Style)

ACAD does not teach you how to destroy your opponent’s speech.

It teaches you how to stress-test their system.

If your attacks do not touch the structure of the other team’s design, you are not attacking in ACAD.

You are commenting.

This part of the manual shows you how to break systems — properly, ethically, and productively.

Because in ACAD, attacks are not aggressive.

They are diagnostic.

21. System Attacks

A system attack asks one question:

If this system were deployed tomorrow, where would it fail first?

Not where it sounds weak.

Where it would collapse.

System attacks always happen on the Action Board.

Never on tone. Never on intentions.

There are four official classes of system attacks in ACAD.

They apply to every round, every sector, every country.

1. Missing actors

This is the most common and the most lethal ACAD attack.

A missing actor is:

a real stakeholder whose behaviour is necessary for the system to function — but is not designed into the architecture.

How to detect a missing actor

Look at their board and ask:

  • who must approve this?

  • who controls access?

  • who enforces compliance?

  • who funds recurring costs?

  • who provides data?

  • who allocates contracts?

If the answer is “implicitly” or “assumed”, you have found a missing actor.

A powerful missing-actor attack sounds like this:

“Your delivery pathway requires accreditation before deployment, but no accrediting body appears in your architecture. Without that actor, your training outputs cannot legally translate into market access.”

That attack is precise.

It is not rhetorical.

Why missing actors are fatal

Because:

systems fail at institutional boundaries.

If you ignore a gatekeeper, your system never enters the system you are trying to change.

2. Broken incentives

This attack asks:

Why would this actor actually behave the way your system needs?

In ACAD, behaviour must be designed.

Not hoped for.

How to detect broken incentives

Pick one key actor from their board and ask:

  • what do they gain?

  • what do they lose?

  • what risk do they take?

  • what workload do they absorb?

If the actor carries cost and risk but receives no structural benefit, the incentive is broken.

Example of a clean incentive attack

“Your system asks private tour operators to mentor and host youth for six months. But your resource model shows no compensation, no recruitment advantage, and no regulatory benefit. This shifts operational cost to firms without incentive alignment.”

That is not an opinion.

That is incentive misalignment.

A critical ACAD principle

Good intentions do not move systems.

Incentives do.

3. Unrealistic assumptions

This attack targets the invisible layer of their system.

How to find assumptions

Scan their delivery and scaling logic and look for statements that depend on:

  • political cooperation,

  • budget approvals,

  • cross-ministerial alignment,

  • private sector engagement,

  • behavioural change.

Then ask:

What must be true for this to work?

If that condition is not designed for, it is an assumption.

A strong assumption attack

“Your model assumes that district tourism offices will actively coordinate youth placements. But your own system map shows these offices have no operational mandate for workforce deployment. That behavioural shift is assumed, not designed.”

This attack exposes structural optimism.

ACAD rule

Assumptions are allowed.

Hidden assumptions are not.

4. Weak feedback loops

This is where ACAD becomes a systems discipline.

This attack asks:

How does this system learn when it is failing?

How to detect weak feedback

Look for:

  • monitoring points

  • performance data flows

  • decision triggers

  • correction mechanisms

If data is collected but nothing changes because of it, the loop is broken.

A proper feedback-loop attack

“You collect employment outcomes quarterly, but no component in your architecture changes training intake, partner selection, or funding allocation based on that data. The system observes failure but cannot correct it.”

This attack targets resilience.

Why feedback loops matter

Because no real system works perfectly at launch.

ACAD rewards designs that anticipate error.

A crucial discipline when attacking

You must always anchor your attack to a specific section of the opponent’s Action Board.

For example:

  • Section 2 – Intervention Architecture

  • Section 3 – Delivery Pathway

  • Section 7 – Scaling Strategy

This keeps ACAD rounds technical, not theatrical.

A short demonstration (tourism example)

Imagine the other team proposes a national youth tourism innovation hub.

A weak attack would be:

“This is expensive and unrealistic.”

An ACAD system attack would be:

“Your hub requires national coordination of private operators, but no industry association or contracting body is present in your architecture. Without a collective contracting mechanism, operator engagement remains voluntary and fragmented, which collapses your scaling pathway.”

Notice:

  • actor identified

  • incentive and coordination problem shown

  • failure point located

That is an ACAD attack.

The difference between criticism and system attack

Criticism says:

“I don’t think this will work.”

System attack says:

“This specific mechanism cannot perform its function under these constraints.”

ACAD only recognises the second.

One final warning

Do not attack what you would personally prefer.

Attack what the system requires.

Your job is not to prove the other team is wrong.

Your job is to show:

where reality pushes back.

That is how ACAD debates remain fair, technical, and genuinely educational.

22. Design Attacks

If system attacks ask:

“Does this system even stand on real institutional ground?”

Design attacks ask a different, equally dangerous question:

“Even if all the right actors exist… is this system designed well?”

Many ACAD teams lose not because they misunderstood the problem — but because they over-designed the solution.

This chapter teaches you how to attack the architecture itself.

Not the idea. Not the intention. The design quality.

In ACAD, design attacks live mainly inside:

Section 2 – Intervention Architecture and are tested through:

Section 3 – Delivery Pathway.

There are three official design attack families.

1. Over-engineering

Over-engineering means:

The system has more structure, components, coordination layers or procedures than the problem actually requires.

How to detect over-engineering

Look at their architecture and ask:

  • How many new units are created?

  • How many interfaces exist between components?

  • How many approval or coordination steps are required?

If you can remove a component and the system still functions, the system is over-engineered.

A classic student trap

Students love to create:

  • national hubs

  • innovation centres

  • coordinating secretariats

  • steering committees

  • multi-stakeholder task forces

All at once.

Each looks reasonable.

Together, they create a fragile, slow, and expensive machine.

A clean over-engineering attack

“Your architecture introduces a national coordination office, a district delivery unit, and an independent monitoring body. Yet the only function these new structures perform is matching youth to existing operators. This could be achieved through direct integration with existing sector associations. Your design multiplies institutions without multiplying system leverage.”

That is a design efficiency attack.

Why over-engineering loses in ACAD

Because ACAD rewards:

  • structural leverage

  • institutional reuse

  • minimal added coordination cost

Not architectural beauty.

2. Duplication

Duplication means:

The system recreates functions that already exist inside the ecosystem.

This is not harmless.

It actively weakens delivery.

How to detect duplication

Look for components that:

  • train skills already trained elsewhere,

  • certify skills already certified,

  • coordinate actors already coordinated,

  • collect data already collected.

Then ask:

Why is this function not integrated instead of replicated?

A proper duplication attack

“Your design introduces a new youth skills certification pathway, while the national vocational authority already certifies tourism qualifications. You have created a parallel legitimacy system rather than integrating into the existing one. This risks rejection by employers and regulatory conflict.”

Duplication is not only inefficient.

It is politically dangerous.

The deeper ACAD logic

Every duplicated function must be justified by:

a structural failure in the existing system.

If that justification is not explicit, the design is weak.

3. Dependency risks

Dependency risk means:

The system’s success depends on one or two fragile components performing perfectly.

This is the design version of single-point-of-failure.

How to detect dependency risk

Ask:

  • Which component, if delayed, stops everything?

  • Which partner, if withdrawn, collapses the pipeline?

  • Which funding source, if removed, freezes operations?

A powerful dependency attack

“Your entire delivery pathway depends on one central digital platform to coordinate placements, certification, and monitoring. If that platform fails to launch on time, none of your other components activate. Your design concentrates operational risk into a single technical dependency.”

This is not a tech attack.

It is an architectural risk attack.

Another form of dependency risk: people

If your system depends on:

  • a national champion,

  • a charismatic coordinator,

  • a single ministry unit,

you must flag this as:

human dependency.

Why dependency risk is deadly in ACAD

Because ACAD explicitly compares:

resilience to failure.

A design that cannot degrade gracefully will always lose to one that can.

A short tourism illustration

Suppose a team proposes:

  • a national tourism youth innovation hub,

  • a digital matching platform,

  • a new certification layer,

  • and a mentorship network.

A strong design attack would be:

“Your system requires the hub, the platform, and the certification layer to all launch before any youth can be placed. This creates a chained dependency architecture. A delayed procurement of the platform alone blocks your entire delivery pathway. A modular design using existing certification and operator associations would activate faster and degrade more safely.”

That is an ACAD design attack.

Design attacks are not simplification for its own sake

Do not confuse ACAD with:

“small is always better”.

Sometimes complex problems need complex systems.

Design attacks only succeed when you prove:

complexity is not doing structural work.

The core test of a good ACAD design

You should always be able to ask:

If we cut this component, what breaks?

If the honest answer is:

“Not much” —

your opponent’s system is over-designed.

And you should attack it.

23. Delivery Attacks

If design attacks test whether the system is well built…

Delivery attacks test whether the system can be run by real people, inside real institutions, under real pressure.

This is where ACAD becomes brutally honest.

Most beautiful systems die here.

Delivery attacks live inside:

  • Section 3 – Delivery Pathway

  • Section 4 – Resource & Constraint Model

You are no longer asking:

“Is this clever?”

You are asking:

“Who is going to survive running this?”

In ACAD, there are three official delivery attack families.

1. Operational overload

Operational overload means:

The system places too many tasks, coordination duties, or reporting requirements on a small set of actors.

How to detect operational overload

Look at one delivery actor on their board and list:

  • how many functions they perform,

  • how many partners they coordinate,

  • how many approvals they process,

  • how much data they manage.

Then ask:

Could this realistically be added to their existing workload?

If the answer is unclear, you have an overload risk.

A clean operational overload attack

“Your delivery pathway assigns district tourism offices responsibility for recruitment, screening, placement coordination, monitoring, and reporting. These offices already operate regulatory and promotional mandates. Your system effectively converts them into workforce agencies without redesigning staffing or budgets.”

This is not an attack on intentions.

It is an attack on operational reality.

Why this matters in ACAD

Because delivery failure is usually caused by:

capacity exhaustion.

Not policy resistance.

2. Human capacity constraints

This attack focuses not on tasks — but on skills.

Human capacity constraints mean:

The system requires specialised competencies that do not exist at scale inside the implementing institutions.

How to detect capacity constraints

Look for roles that require:

  • digital system management

  • contract negotiation

  • data analytics

  • programme design

  • quality assurance

Then ask:

Who in the delivery institutions currently performs this function?

If the answer is “we will train them”, that is not capacity.

That is another dependency.

A strong human capacity attack

“Your model assumes that school administrators can manage employer contracting and placement tracking. There is no evidence in your resource model that these competencies exist or that a professional transition pathway is designed. This converts an education workforce into a labour market intermediary without capacity reengineering.”

This attack isolates skill mismatches.

ACAD principle

Training is not capacity.

Capacity is:

existing institutional ability to perform specialised tasks reliably.

3. Political barriers

This is the most sensitive and most powerful delivery attack.

Political barriers mean:

The system requires decisions that are politically costly, institutionally threatening, or strategically unattractive to key actors.

How to detect political barriers

Look for design elements that:

  • shift budgets between institutions,

  • reallocate authority,

  • weaken existing mandates,

  • expose performance failures,

  • centralise or decentralise control.

Then ask:

Who loses power if this system succeeds?

If someone loses power, your system must manage that conflict.

A precise political barrier attack

“Your design requires the tourism board to transfer certification authority to a new youth unit. This reduces institutional control over standards and revenue. You do not include any political or administrative mechanism to manage that loss of authority.”

That is a political feasibility failure.

ACAD does not punish political difficulty

It punishes political blindness.

A short tourism illustration

Imagine a team proposes a national youth tourism placement authority.

A delivery attack would be:

“Your authority absorbs placement powers currently exercised by private associations and district offices. That reallocation creates institutional resistance. Your delivery pathway contains no political negotiation, legal transition, or compensation mechanism.”

This attack is not cynical.

It is realistic.

The discipline of delivery attacks

You must never say:

“This is politically unrealistic.”

You must say:

“This design reallocates authority in this way, affecting this actor, without any transition mechanism.”

Delivery attacks must always identify:

  • the affected institution,

  • the shifted responsibility,

  • the missing transition logic.

Why delivery attacks matter more than design attacks

Because:

design flaws can be fixed.

Delivery impossibility cannot.

The ACAD delivery attack test

A delivery attack succeeds if you can show:

even a well-designed system will stall at implementation.

That is the deepest level of critique in ACAD.

And it is where the strongest teams win.

PART IX – Attacking Solutions (ACAD Style)

In ACAD, attacks are not about speakers, intentions, or niceness of the idea. They are about whether the proposed system can actually function, survive and scale in the real world.

Your job is to break the logic of the system shown on the Action Board.

21. System Attacks

System attacks focus on the structure of the problem and the environment in which the solution operates.

You are testing whether the team has correctly understood the real system.

a) Missing actors

You challenge whether the team has left out any critical stakeholder that can block, distort or hijack the intervention.

Examples of missing actors include:

  • regulators and ministries

  • local authorities

  • gatekeepers (school heads, community leaders, hospital directors, cooperatives, etc.)

  • informal power holders

  • competitors or substitute providers

A strong system attack sounds like:

“Your system map does not include the district authority that approves operations. Without them, the delivery pathway cannot legally begin.”

This attack shows that the team’s system is incomplete, not merely unclear.

b) Broken incentives

Here you examine whether the people inside the system actually have a reason to behave the way the solution assumes they will.

You test:

  • Who benefits?

  • Who loses?

  • Who has extra work?

  • Who controls resources?

A broken-incentive attack shows that: even if the solution exists, actors are not motivated to cooperate.

For example:

  • teachers gain no benefit from extra reporting

  • community agents are unpaid

  • local offices lose authority under the new system

A strong ACAD attack:

“Your model assumes frontline workers will adopt your workflow, yet you provide no incentive—financial, institutional or reputational—for them to do so.”

c) Unrealistic assumptions

Here you identify assumptions that are not supported by context or evidence.

Typical unrealistic assumptions include:

  • digital literacy is widespread

  • trust already exists

  • data is easily available

  • communities are homogeneous

  • partners will cooperate automatically

You do not say “this is optimistic”. You show that the system requires conditions that do not exist.

Example:

“Your intervention assumes reliable connectivity at all delivery points, yet your resource model does not account for connectivity infrastructure.”

d) Weak or absent feedback loops

ACAD systems must show how failure, under-performance or misuse is detected and corrected.

You attack:

  • absence of monitoring triggers

  • no learning loops

  • no mechanism to adapt the design

A strong attack:

“Once your programme starts under-performing, where in your system does corrective decision-making occur?”

If there is no loop, the system is static and fragile.

22. Design Attacks

Design attacks focus on the internal architecture of the solution itself.

You are testing whether the proposed components form a coherent and efficient system.

a) Over-engineering

This challenges solutions that are unnecessarily complex for the problem.

You identify:

  • too many platforms

  • too many intermediaries

  • too many layers of approval

  • too many tools performing similar functions

A strong design attack:

“Your architecture introduces four operational layers where one delivery layer would achieve the same outcome.”

The harm:

  • higher failure probability

  • higher cost

  • slower deployment

  • higher training burden

b) Duplication

Here you show that the solution recreates what already exists in the system.

You attack:

  • parallel data systems

  • parallel delivery structures

  • parallel coordination bodies

A strong ACAD attack:

“Your design duplicates an existing service channel rather than integrating into it, creating competition for the same users and staff.”

This weakens:

  • institutional alignment

  • sustainability

  • political acceptability

c) Dependency risks

This examines whether the entire solution relies on:

  • a single funder

  • a single technology provider

  • one key individual

  • one international partner

A strong design attack:

“Your intervention collapses if this single partner withdraws, yet your architecture contains no redundancy or fallback pathway.”

High dependency means: the system is not resilient.

23. Delivery Attacks

Delivery attacks focus on whether the solution can actually be implemented in the real world.

This is the operational reality check.

a) Operational overload

Here you test whether the delivery plan creates:

  • excessive reporting

  • excessive coordination

  • excessive supervision requirements

  • excessive logistics

A strong attack:

“Your delivery pathway requires weekly reporting, multi-stakeholder coordination and on-site supervision, yet your operational team consists of two staff.”

This shows: The delivery system is structurally overloaded.

b) Human capacity constraints

This examines whether the people required to deliver the solution:

  • exist in sufficient numbers

  • have the required skills

  • can be retained

A strong ACAD attack:

“Your model requires trained facilitators in every location, but your resource model provides no pipeline for recruitment, training or retention.”

If human capacity is missing, the system is theoretically elegant but practically impossible.

c) Political and institutional barriers

You test:

  • regulatory permissions

  • mandates of public institutions

  • alignment with national or local policy

  • authority conflicts

A strong attack:

“Your delivery pathway assumes direct access to public facilities, yet no institutional mandate or Mo U mechanism is shown on your board.”

This demonstrates: the system is blocked before delivery even begins.

24. Impact Attacks

Impact attacks examine whether the solution’s claimed results are credible, meaningful and scalable.

a) Weak indicators

You attack the measurement framework by testing whether indicators:

  • actually reflect change

  • capture outcomes rather than activity

  • can be collected reliably

A strong attack:

“Your indicators measure participation and attendance, but not behavioural or economic change.”

This exposes: a gap between activity and impact.

b) Inflated projections

Here you challenge growth, reach and impact numbers that:

  • assume linear expansion

  • ignore resource limits

  • ignore operational friction

A strong ACAD attack:

“Your scaling projection increases coverage tenfold without increasing staff, budget or infrastructure.”

This shows that: their impact model is mathematically inconsistent with their resource model.

c) Non-scalable models

You identify features that work only at small scale, such as:

  • intensive mentoring

  • high-touch supervision

  • custom local partnerships

  • manual coordination

A strong attack:

“Your delivery model relies on high-touch facilitation that cannot be maintained when moving from pilot to regional scale.”

This directly undermines: their replicability and reach claims.

Core ACAD principle for attacks (for you as a debater)

Every attack must link back to at least one of the official ACAD weighing lenses:

  • reach

  • depth

  • sustainability

  • replicability

  • institutional alignment

If your attack does not weaken one of these, it is not a real ACAD attack — it is just criticism.

Below is a fully expanded and ACAD-accurate version of PART X – Cross-Examination Mastery, written for how cross-ex actually functions inside the Action Board logic (not traditional debate).

PART X – Cross-Examination Mastery

In ACAD, cross-examination is not a trap-setting exercise. It is a structured diagnostic tool to stress-test the opponent’s system.

Your goal is not to make them look weak. Your goal is to reveal whether their system can function, survive, and scale.

25. Asking ACAD Questions

ACAD questions must always be anchored to something that already appears on the Action Board.

You never ask abstract questions. You interrogate a specific part of their system design.

a) How to expose system gaps

Here, you target their Problem System Map and their Intervention Architecture.

Your objective is to discover whether the system is:

  • incomplete

  • oversimplified

  • missing critical power or control nodes

You should ask questions that force them to identify:

  • who authorises

  • who blocks

  • who controls resources

  • who enforces rules

  • who changes behaviour

Strong ACAD-style questions look like:

“Which actor in your system has the authority to approve deployment at the community level?”

“Who enforces compliance when one delivery actor does not perform?”

“Which actor owns the data and who has decision rights over it?”

What this exposes: If they hesitate, generalise, or introduce a new actor not shown on the board, you have revealed a system gap.

A powerful ACAD habit: → whenever they say “partners”, “stakeholders”, or “the community”, ask:

“Which specific actor performs this role in your system?”

b) How to test feasibility

Feasibility in ACAD is not about whether something is “possible”.

It is about whether the delivery system can operate with the resources, people and institutions shown on the board.

You should connect three sections:

  • Delivery Pathway

  • Resource & Constraint Model

  • Ownership and Responsibilities

Strong feasibility-testing questions:

“Who exactly is responsible for this step, and is this role already funded in your resource model?”

“How many personnel are required per site, and where are they sourced from?”

“Which organisation legally hosts this operation?”

These questions force them to reconcile:

  • operational steps with

  • real capacity

A very effective ACAD pattern:

“You show this activity in the delivery pathway. Where is the corresponding resource for it in your resource model?”

If the resource does not exist → feasibility collapses.

c) How to test scale logic

Scaling in ACAD is not simply “doing more of the same”.

You must test whether their system:

  • structurally supports expansion

  • institutionally supports replication

  • financially supports growth

You should link:

  • Scaling & Replication Strategy with

  • Resource & Constraint Model with

  • Delivery Architecture

Strong scale-testing questions:

“What changes in your architecture when you move from one district to ten districts?”

“Which components must be replicated and which can be shared?”

“Which institution becomes the scaling owner at national level?”

Another powerful question:

“Which constraint becomes binding at scale—human capacity, funding, regulation, or infrastructure?”

If they cannot identify the new binding constraint, they do not actually understand their scaling pathway.

Important ACAD rule for asking questions

You must always know what failure would look like before you ask the question.

If you cannot identify which part of the board would collapse when they fail to answer, the question is not strategic.

26. Answering ACAD Questions

In ACAD, answers are not speeches. They are design clarifications and system defences.

a) Evidence-backed responses

Every answer must be anchored to:

  • your board

  • your evidence

  • your operational logic

Strong ACAD answers always follow this structure:

  • Point to the system element

  • Explain its function

  • Justify it using evidence or contextual logic

Example structure:

“This step is owned by the district education office, as shown in our delivery pathway. This office already manages similar programmes, and our pilot data shows they can absorb this workload.”

What counts as acceptable evidence:

  • pilot or field data

  • operational experience

  • comparable programme performance

  • contextual studies

  • credible sector reports

What does not count:

  • general optimism

  • international success stories without contextual alignment

  • personal belief

b) Admitting uncertainty properly

In ACAD, admitting uncertainty is not weakness.

What matters is whether uncertainty is:

  • anticipated

  • monitored

  • managed

A strong ACAD uncertainty admission looks like:

“We are uncertain about uptake rates in the first three months. For that reason, our measurement framework includes weekly adoption tracking and a redesign trigger after month two.”

You must always attach uncertainty to:

  • a monitoring mechanism and

  • a decision mechanism

Never say:

“We are not sure, but we will see.”

Instead say:

“We are not certain, therefore we have designed a feedback and adjustment loop.”

This preserves:

  • system credibility

  • resilience

  • learning capacity

c) Redesigning under pressure

This is one of the most important ACAD skills.

In cross-ex, you are allowed to refine your system — but you must do so without breaking internal logic.

Good redesign under pressure has three properties:

  • It changes one component

  • It preserves the overall architecture

  • It respects existing constraints

A strong redesign response looks like:

“If institutional access becomes restricted, we shift delivery ownership from schools to community centres, while keeping the same training and monitoring architecture.”

What you are demonstrating:

  • modularity of design

  • adaptability

  • operational realism

What you must never do:

  • introduce entirely new partners without showing where they fit

  • invent new funding streams without integrating them into the resource model

  • add new activities without adjusting capacity

Critical rule when redesigning

Every redesign must still satisfy the ACAD weighing lenses:

  • reach

  • depth

  • sustainability

  • replicability

  • institutional alignment

If your redesign improves one but destroys another, you must acknowledge and manage that trade-off.

Practical note for you (as a system-focused debater)

Your strength — especially from your work in youth platforms, education systems and multi-stakeholder programmes — is operational realism.

In cross-ex:

  • think like a programme manager,

  • not like a debater.

Ask:

“Who runs this on Monday morning?” “Who pays for it?” “Who signs the approval?” “Who fixes it when it breaks?”

That mindset is exactly what ACAD judges are evaluating.

PART XI – Rebuttals in ACAD

27. What Rebuttal Means in ACAD

If you come to ACAD from traditional debate, this is the moment where your instincts must change.

In most formats, rebuttal means: repeat your case, defend your points, attack their points, sound convincing.

In ACAD, rebuttal means something far stricter and far more powerful:

You are not defending a position. You are collapsing a comparison between two systems.

Think of the entire debate as a long design review meeting. By the time rebuttals start, both systems already exist. Both Action Boards are already on the table.

Your job is not to describe your system again.

Your job is to answer one single silent question in the Evaluator’s mind:

Which system should I trust to actually work, scale, and survive reality?

Rebuttal in ACAD is not a speech.

It is a final engineering decision.

27.1 The three absolute rules of ACAD rebuttals

Rule 1 — No repetition

If something is already on your Action Board, and it has already been explained by a speaker,

you never repeat it.

Not even in short form.

Not even for clarity.

Because in ACAD, repeating design is a waste of evaluation time.

The Evaluator is not checking whether your system exists. They are checking whether it beats the other system.

So instead of saying:

“Our delivery pathway is strong because we have trained facilitators, local partners, and a monitoring system…”

You must say:

“Compared to their delivery pathway, ours requires one institutional approval point instead of three — which removes their main political bottleneck.”

Repetition describes. Rebuttal discriminates.

Rule 2 — No restating your board

This is the most common mistake even strong debaters make.

They walk back through their own architecture.

They point again to their actors.

They re-explain their logic.

In ACAD, that is not a rebuttal.

It is a delayed constructive.

Your board already speaks for itself.

In rebuttal, you only speak about your board when it is used as a benchmark against the other team.

Your board is not a story.

Your board is a measuring instrument.

Rule 3 — Only comparison and collapse

Every sentence in an ACAD rebuttal must do at least one of two things:

  • compare systems, or

  • collapse the decision.

If a sentence does not clearly move the judge toward a decision, it does not belong in rebuttal.

27.2 What “comparison” actually means in ACAD

Comparison in ACAD is not: “we are better than them.”

It is: placing two systems under the same stress test.

You must compare them using the official ACAD lenses:

  • system robustness

  • delivery feasibility

  • speed to impact

  • depth of impact

  • scalability

  • resilience to failure

But comparison must be concrete.

Not abstract.

Not rhetorical.

Example – weak comparison

“Our system is more scalable than theirs.”

This tells the Evaluator nothing.

Example – ACAD comparison

“Their scale depends on retraining a new workforce in every district. Our scale depends on extending an existing institutional workflow. At national expansion, human capacity becomes their binding constraint — not ours.”

Notice what happened:

You did not praise your design.

You diagnosed their failure point.

Then you positioned your system next to that failure.

In ACAD, the other team’s weakness is the strongest evidence for your superiority.

27.3 What “collapse” really means

Collapse is the most misunderstood concept in ACAD.

Collapse does not mean: “summarise the debate.”

Collapse means:

reducing the entire debate into a small number of decisive system differences.

Think like this:

If the Evaluator could only remember two things after your speech, what must those two things be?

Those two things are your collapses.

A real collapse sounds like this:

“This debate comes down to delivery ownership and scaling control.”

Then you prove that:

  • one team controls delivery inside existing institutions

  • the other relies on informal coordination

  • one can scale through mandate

  • the other must renegotiate every expansion

That is collapse.

Not coverage. Not listing.

Decision compression.

27.4 The hidden structure of a perfect ACAD rebuttal

Every high-quality ACAD rebuttal follows this internal structure:

Step 1 – Name the decisive lens

You explicitly tell the Evaluator what they should judge on.

For example:

“The decisive question in this debate is delivery feasibility.”

or

“The decisive difference is scalability under institutional constraints.”

Step 2 – Compare both systems on that lens

You must speak about both teams.

Always.

Never talk only about the other team.

Never talk only about yourself.

ACAD rebuttal logic is always symmetrical:

  • their model

  • your model

  • same test

Step 3 – Identify the breaking point

You show where one system stops functioning.

A breaking point can be:

  • an actor without authority

  • a missing resource

  • a political veto

  • a capacity ceiling

  • a regulatory barrier

  • an unowned workflow

Step 4 – Show why your system survives that same stress

This is not self-praise.

It is structural survival.

Step 5 – Collapse the decision

You end the section by telling the Evaluator:

“Because of this, the only system that remains feasible at scale is ours.”

Not emotionally.

Not rhetorically.

Logically.

27.5 How ACAD rebuttals use attacks from earlier parts

In traditional formats, rebuttals often introduce new arguments.

In ACAD, rebuttals almost never introduce new attacks.

They activate the attacks that already happened.

Earlier in the debate:

  • system attacks revealed missing actors

  • design attacks revealed over-engineering

  • delivery attacks revealed capacity problems

  • impact attacks revealed inflated projections

Rebuttal is where you connect those attacks to outcomes.

For example:

Earlier:

“They never showed who maintains the digital infrastructure.”

Rebuttal:

“Because maintenance ownership is missing, their delivery pathway collapses after the pilot phase — and this directly explains why their scaling plan is non-credible.”

You are no longer arguing.

You are explaining consequences.

27.6 What you must never do in an ACAD rebuttal

Never:

  • introduce a new programme idea

  • add a new actor to your system

  • invent a new funding source

  • redesign a delivery pathway

  • add a new impact indicator

Rebuttal is not design time.

Design happens in constructives and cross-ex.

Rebuttal is decision time.

27.7 How ACAD rebuttals treat evidence

In rebuttal, evidence is not quoted.

It is referenced as validation.

You do not say:

“According to a 2022 report…”

You say:

“Their cost model assumes per-beneficiary costs that contradict sector benchmarks, while ours is aligned with operational averages shown in our evidence base.”

The Evaluator already saw your sources.

Rebuttal uses evidence to confirm credibility — not to build a new case.

27.8 The mental shift you must make

This is especially important for you, Ismael.

Given your background in building youth programmes, platforms and multi-actor initiatives, your instinct is already very close to ACAD.

In rebuttal, imagine that:

You are sitting in front of a real funding committee.

Two proposals are on the table.

Your role is to help the committee avoid choosing a system that will fail after the launch photo.

So instead of thinking:

“How do I win this debate?”

Think:

“Which system would I approve if this were my organisation’s money, staff and reputation?”

That is the correct ACAD mindset.

27.9 A short example of a complete ACAD rebuttal section

Let us model a full mini-collapse.

“The decisive issue in this debate is delivery feasibility under institutional constraints. Their system depends on school administrators volunteering additional time without budget reallocation. That creates a human-capacity ceiling after the pilot phase. Our system embeds delivery inside the district youth office’s existing mandate, with dedicated operational roles already budgeted. Under the same scale test — moving from one district to ten — their system must renegotiate participation, while ours expands through administrative extension. Because of this, only our system remains deliverable beyond pilot level.”

Notice:

  • one lens

  • both systems

  • one breaking point

  • one survival

  • one decision

That is an ACAD rebuttal.

Final sentence you should always remember

In ACAD, rebuttal is not the art of speaking well. It is the art of helping the Evaluator choose the system that will not fail.

28. Impact Lead Closing Method

The Impact Lead Closing is the pinnacle of the ACAD debate. After all the systems have been presented, analyzed, cross-examined, and stressed, it is the Impact Lead’s job to crystallize the decision for the Evaluator.

Unlike a traditional debate closer that might rely on eloquence, charisma, or emotional appeal, the ACAD closer is all about clarity, logic, and comparison.

28.1 The Core Philosophy

Two principles govern the Impact Lead Closing:

  • Collapse the debate to its decisive differences

  • The Evaluator should leave the room remembering only the points that truly separate the two systems.

  • These points are usually the breaking points revealed by system, design, delivery, or impact attacks.

  • Demonstrate comparative superiority

  • You are not defending your system abstractly.

  • You are explicitly showing why your system survives stress tests better than your opponent’s.

28.2 Step 1 – Identify the two decisive system advantages

A strong Impact Lead starts by scanning the debate and asking:

“Which two system characteristics truly decide feasibility, scale, or impact?”

Typically, these may be:

  • Delivery robustness – who actually can run the system with existing capacity?

  • Scalability – who can grow their system without breaking it?

Other examples:

  • speed to impact

  • institutional alignment

  • measurement credibility

  • resilience to failure

You must pick only the two lenses that matter most. Too many and the Evaluator is confused. Too few and you risk missing a key weakness in the other system.

Example:

“After examining both Action Boards and listening to cross-ex, the decisive differences are: delivery feasibility and scaling capacity.”

28.3 Step 2 – Compare both systems on these decisive advantages

This is where the board becomes your argument.

For each decisive advantage:

  • Show how your system survives stress tests.

  • Show where the opposing system collapses.

Do this clearly and quantitatively where possible.

Example – Delivery Feasibility:

“Their delivery pathway relies on overworked district offices without additional staff or budget, creating a human-capacity ceiling. Our system embeds delivery into an existing youth mandate with trained personnel and dedicated funding. Under operational stress, only our pathway remains feasible.”

Example – Scalability:

“Their expansion requires retraining local facilitators in every district, causing exponential resource demand. Our system extends through institutional workflows already budgeted for growth. Thus, at national scale, only our model is replicable.”

28.4 Step 3 – Show comparative superiority

After comparing, you must synthesize the outcome in one crisp statement that signals to the Evaluator which system they must choose.

Good comparative statements:

  • “Because of these two decisive advantages, only our system reliably delivers impact at scale.”

  • “Considering both feasibility and replicability, our design withstands operational realities that collapse theirs.”

  • “On both delivery and scaling, our system is robust; theirs is vulnerable. The choice is clear.”

28.5 Step 4 – Optional tie-in to ACAD weighing lenses

You can anchor your closing to the official ACAD evaluation framework:

  • reach

  • depth

  • sustainability

  • replicability

  • institutional alignment

This reinforces to Evaluators that your collapse is logical, standardized, and measurable, not rhetorical.

Example:

“On the ACAD lenses of sustainability and replicability, only our system maintains outputs under operational and scaling stress. Their system fails both.”

28.6 Practical tips for Impact Leads

  • Start with the decisive lenses – don’t introduce new issues.

  • Use comparative phrasing – always place the two systems side by side.

  • Quantify or visualize – show numbers, timelines, or pathways from the Action Board.

  • Collapse efficiently – no new arguments, no repetition, no storytelling.

  • End with clarity – the Evaluator should immediately know which system is superior.

Quick Example Closing Flow:

  • “The decisive advantages are delivery feasibility and scalability.”

  • Compare delivery: “They overwork existing offices; we embed in existing mandate.”

  • Compare scalability: “They retrain staff; we use institutional workflow extension.”

  • Conclude: “Therefore, only our system reliably delivers impact at scale. Decision is clear.”

Remember: The Impact Lead is the last voice the Evaluator hears. A strong closing compresses the debate, shows clear comparative superiority, and leaves no ambiguity about which system survives the real-world stress test.

This is where ACAD becomes actionable, measurable, and memorable.

PART XII – Delivery & Communication

ACAD is a visual, logical, and operational debate. Your words are only effective if they communicate the system clearly. In this part, we focus on how to deliver your argument so that Evaluators immediately understand your Action Board, your comparisons, and your system’s survival under stress.

29. Speaking Style in ACAD

ACAD speaking style is different from traditional debate styles. You are not persuading a lay audience with charisma or rhetoric. You are guiding an Evaluator through a complex system and proving your system’s feasibility, scalability, and impact.

The Three Pillars of ACAD Delivery

Pillar Focus Strategic Purpose
Clarity Simple, precise, jargon-free Eliminates ambiguity; ensures the system logic is immediately traceable.
Structure Action Board alignment Organizes complex information into a logical Problem → Design → Delivery flow.
Logic Evidence-backed claims Prioritizes operational feasibility over persuasive flair or emotional appeals.

Example:

Weak: “Our system is clearly superior and will outperform any other model.” Strong: “Their delivery requires weekly site visits by external facilitators, creating a capacity ceiling. Our delivery uses existing institutional staff, allowing continuous operation at scale.”

Additional Delivery Guidelines

  • Reference the board constantly

  • Point to diagrams, arrows, or numbers.

  • Make the visual evidence do half the work.

  • Maintain authority without aggression

  • Speak confidently about your system; avoid attacking opponents emotionally.

  • Let comparisons and collapses do the judgment work.

  • Use measurable language

  • “Covers 10 districts in 6 months” beats “will expand quickly.”

  • Always quantify reach, time, capacity, or cost when possible.

  • Responding under pressure

  • If a question arises mid-speech, pause, locate the relevant board section, and reference it.

  • Avoid improvising entirely new elements; work with your existing architecture.

Summary of ACAD Speaking Style

Principle Focus Example
Clarity Make the system instantly understandable “Activity A → Output B → Outcome C”
Structure Logical, sequenced presentation Problem → Design → Delivery → Impact
Logic Evidence-based comparison, not persuasion “Their system fails X; ours survives Y”

Remember: In ACAD, delivery is a tool for understanding, not performance. The stronger your communication, the more clearly the Evaluator sees which system will survive, scale, and create impact.

Every word should guide, point, and compare, never persuade for persuasion’s sake.

This mindset keeps the debate action-focused, measurable, and true to ACAD culture.

30. Using Visuals Properly

In ACAD, your Action Board is the backbone of your argument. It is not decoration; it is the system itself. How you present it visually can make or break comprehension for the Evaluator. Poor visuals lead to confusion, misunderstandings, and lost points—even if your system is brilliant.

30.1 Layout Rules

  • Logical Flow

  • The board should mirror the system’s operation: Problem → Design → Delivery → Impact → Scaling → Risks.

  • Use arrows, numbered steps, or modular boxes to show relationships between components.

  • Consistent Sections

  • Each board must have the same core sections, even if the content differs.

  • This ensures Evaluators can quickly navigate any team’s system.

  • Hierarchy and Emphasis

  • Highlight key decision points, bottlenecks, or breaking points.

  • Use bold boxes, borders, or color codes to indicate critical nodes vs supporting elements.

  • White Space

  • Avoid clutter. Empty space helps the eye follow the flow.

  • Dense visuals with overlapping boxes make evaluation slow and error-prone.

30.2 Font, Color, and Readability

  • Fonts

  • Use clean, sans-serif fonts (e.g., Arial, Helvetica) for clarity.

  • Minimum font size: 12 pt for general content, 14–16 pt for headings.

  • Color Coding

  • Use colors consistently across similar functions:

  • Actors → blue

  • Activities → green

  • Outcomes → orange

  • Risks → red

  • Avoid overloading the board with too many colors. Stick to 3–5 core colors.

  • Contrast

  • Ensure text stands out against background.

  • Light text on dark background OR dark text on light background.

30.3 Visual Integrity

  • Accuracy over decoration

  • Diagrams must match your system exactly.

  • No arrows pointing to nothing, no steps out of order, no fake connections.

  • Consistency with speech

  • Every element on the board must correspond to what you say.

  • Never reference something that does not exist visually.

  • Traceable logic

  • Evaluators should be able to follow the board from start to finish without hearing a single word and understand the system.

  • Highlight comparative points

  • When making comparisons in rebuttals, use call-outs or markers to show differences between your board and the opponent’s.

  • Example: “Our bottleneck is here (green), theirs is here (red)”

30.4 Practical Example of Good Visual Design

  • Problem System Map: clearly shows actors, flows, and bottlenecks with arrows.

  • Intervention Architecture: boxes for each component, lines showing interaction, responsibilities labeled.

  • Delivery Pathway: numbered steps with timelines.

  • Impact Logic Chain: stacked boxes showing Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact.

  • Scaling & Risk: side panel highlighting scaling steps, binding constraints, and mitigation measures.

30.5 Common Visual Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It Fails ACAD-Friendly Fix
Cluttered board Hard to follow Use hierarchy, white space, modular boxes
Overuse of colors Distracts Limit to 3–5 colors, keep consistent meaning
Misaligned arrows or lines Confuses logic Double-check connections, use straight lines, consistent flow
Tiny text Unreadable Minimum 12 pt; highlight headings 14–16 pt
Non-matching speech Evaluator confusion Always reference exact board elements while speaking

Key Takeaways

  • Your board is a map of reality, not decoration.

  • Visuals must be intuitive, accurate, and comparable.

  • Evaluators should instantly see: who does what, where the bottlenecks are, and which system survives stress tests.

  • When done correctly, visuals amplify your speech, reduce cognitive load, and make the ACAD debate actionable and measurable.

PART XIII – Judging ACAD

Judging in ACAD is fundamentally different from judging traditional debate formats. Evaluators are not listening for clever rhetoric, memorable speeches, or emotional persuasion. They are evaluating systems, not orators.

This part serves as the Impact Panel Manual — the official guide for how judges should observe, compare, and decide.

31. The Impact Panel Manual

31.1 What Judges Must Evaluate

Evaluators focus exclusively on the operational and comparative reality of the systems presented. Their evaluation is structured through ACAD’s official weighing lenses:

  • System Robustness

  • Does the system survive stress tests?

  • Are the actors, flows, and interventions functional under real-world conditions?

  • Delivery Feasibility

  • Can the system actually be implemented given time, resources, and existing institutional structures?

  • Are there clear ownership and accountability pathways?

  • Impact Depth & Credibility

  • Are the projected outputs, outcomes, and impacts realistic and measurable?

  • Does the system address root causes or just surface problems?

  • Scalability & Replicability

  • Can the system grow geographically, demographically, or institutionally without failure?

  • Are the scaling mechanisms embedded within operational reality?

  • Resilience & Risk Mitigation

  • Does the system account for failures, bottlenecks, and external shocks?

  • Are mitigation strategies well-defined and practical?

  • Comparative Superiority

  • How does each system perform relative to the other under these lenses?

  • Which system survives the stress tests better?

31.2 What Judges Must Ignore

To maintain ACAD’s culture and objectivity, evaluators must consciously ignore factors that do not reflect system performance:

  • Rhetoric & Eloquence

  • Speaking style, charisma, and theatricality are irrelevant.

  • Evaluators focus on the content and clarity of systems, not delivery flair.

  • Repetition or Volume of Information

  • More slides or more words do not equal superiority.

  • Judges assess logic, completeness, and clarity, not density of argument.

  • Emotional Appeals

  • Passionate appeals to morality, pity, or excitement have no bearing.

  • Ad Hominem or Opponent Attacks

  • Judges evaluate systems, not debaters. Personal attacks are out of bounds.

  • Innovation Claims Without Evidence

  • Just claiming a solution is “innovative” is meaningless without operational proof or measurable outcomes.

31.3 Judge Workflow During an ACAD Debate

  • Observe

  • Follow the Action Board while speakers present.

  • Map system actors, flows, interventions, delivery, and impact in your notes.

  • Cross-Reference

  • Use cross-examination to verify assumptions, logic, and feasibility.

  • Identify breaking points or potential failures.

  • Compare Systems

  • Apply the official weighing lenses consistently.

  • Explicitly mark which system performs better under each lens.

  • Identify Decisive Lenses

  • Determine 1–2 critical advantages that will decide the outcome.

  • Look for survival points under real-world constraints.

  • Decide

  • Declare the system that is more robust, feasible, scalable, and impactful as the winner.

  • Judges should provide reasoned justification, referencing evidence, board logic, and comparison — not style or delivery.

31.4 Scoring & Notes

  • Notes must align with ACAD principles: system robustness, delivery feasibility, impact depth, scalability, and risk management.

  • Optional: score each lens (1–5) for clarity, but final judgment is holistic, not mechanical.

  • Evaluators should be able to recreate the decision from their notes alone, independent of speeches.

31.5 The ACAD Judge Mindset

Think of yourself as a real-world funding committee or operational director:

“If I had to deploy these systems tomorrow, which one would work, scale, and survive the stresses of reality?”

  • You are not rewarding cleverness.

  • You are not rewarding verbosity.

  • You are rewarding functionality, clarity, and comparative advantage.

This mindset ensures that ACAD debates remain practical, action-driven, and credible.

32. Official ACAD Ballot

The Official ACAD Ballot is the Evaluator’s tool for recording observations, comparing systems, and deciding the winner. Unlike traditional debate scoring sheets, the ACAD ballot focuses exclusively on system performance, design logic, operational feasibility, impact, and scalability.

It ensures consistency, transparency, and alignment with ACAD culture.

32.1 Scoring Dimensions

The ballot is divided into six primary dimensions. Each dimension is evaluated objectively, based on evidence, Action Board logic, and cross-examination findings.

1. System Quality

  • How well does the proposed system map the problem space?

  • Are all key actors, flows, and feedback loops accounted for?

  • Are root causes addressed rather than just surface symptoms?

2. Design Quality

  • Are interventions well-structured, coherent, and logically connected?

  • Does the architecture avoid duplication, over-engineering, or dependency pitfalls?

  • Are the roles, responsibilities, and workflows clearly assigned?

3. Delivery Feasibility

  • Can the system realistically be implemented?

  • Are operational requirements, timelines, and human capacity aligned with local constraints?

  • Are ownership and accountability clearly defined?

4. Impact Credibility

  • Are outputs, outcomes, and impacts realistic, measurable, and evidence-backed?

  • Does the system create meaningful, lasting change rather than superficial effects?

5. Scalability Strength

  • Can the system grow geographically, demographically, or institutionally without collapsing?

  • Are scaling pathways clearly defined and tested against stress points?

  • Are mitigation strategies in place for potential bottlenecks?

6. Comparative Analysis

  • How does this system perform relative to the opponent under each lens?

  • Are weaknesses of the other system exposed and explained?

  • Are decisive lenses identified and collapsed for the final judgment?

32.2 How to Use the ACAD Ballot

  • Observe & Note

  • Track each system using the Action Board as your guide.

  • Note strengths, weaknesses, and evidence in each dimension.

  • Score Each Dimension

  • Optional numerical score (e.g., 1–5) or descriptive evaluation.

  • Focus on operational truth, not style or speech delivery.

  • Compare Systems Directly

  • For each dimension, indicate which system performs better.

  • Highlight decisive advantages that will inform the final judgment.

  • Collapse the Decision

  • Identify 1–2 key lenses that determine the winner.

  • Summarize why one system is superior based on robust evidence and logic.

  • Write Reasoned Justification

  • Even if the scoring is numeric, include brief explanatory notes referencing board logic and comparison points.

  • This ensures transparency and allows teams to understand the rationale.

32.3 Sample ACAD Ballot Layout

Dimension System A Evaluation System B Evaluation Winner / Notes
System Quality 4/5 – actors & flows complete 3/5 – missing key actors A
Design Quality 5/5 – coherent, logical 3/5 – over-engineered A
Delivery Feasibility 4/5 – feasible within constraints 2/5 – operational bottlenecks A
Impact Credibility 4/5 – realistic outcomes 3/5 – inflated projections A
Scalability Strength 5/5 – institutional pathways 2/5 – weak scaling plan A
Comparative Analysis A clearly outperforms B – A

Final Decision: System A wins – decisive in delivery and scalability.

Key Takeaways:

  • The ACAD ballot keeps judging consistent and objective.

  • Evaluators focus on systems, not speeches.

  • Collapsing and comparing across lenses ensures decisions are logical, evidence-based, and reproducible.

33. How Decisions Are Written

In ACAD, writing decisions is just as important as making them. The goal is to clearly communicate the rationale behind the judgment, so teams understand why one system outperformed the other, and so that future Evaluators can replicate the logic if needed.

ACAD decisions are evidence-driven, comparative, and concise, avoiding any rhetoric, praise, or style-based commentary.

33.1 The ACAD Decision Structure

An effective ACAD decision follows a three-part structure:

Step 1 – State the Winner

  • Begin with a clear declaration of which system is chosen.

  • Example:

“System A is the winning solution.”

  • Avoid ambiguous language like “System A performed slightly better,” which weakens the clarity of your judgment.

Step 2 – Identify the Decisive Lenses

  • Explicitly mention the 1–2 critical dimensions that determined the outcome.

  • These are the lenses where one system clearly outperforms the other.

  • Example:

“The decisive factors are delivery feasibility and scalability strength.”

  • Only list the most critical factors; avoid enumerating every minor strength or weakness.

Step 3 – Provide Comparative Justification

  • Explain why the winning system is superior under these lenses.

  • Reference evidence, logic, and the Action Board, not style or speeches.

  • Structure justification using a simple formula:

“System A surpasses System B in [Lens 1] because [specific evidence or board logic]. In [Lens 2], System A is stronger because [specific evidence or board logic]. Therefore, System A is the superior system overall.”

  • Use clear, measurable, or visual examples when possible.

33.2 Example ACAD Decision

Winner: System A Decisive Lenses: Delivery feasibility, Scalability strength Justification: “System A’s delivery integrates into existing institutional workflows with trained personnel, while System B relies on external volunteers, creating operational bottlenecks. System A also scales through pre-defined pathways with minimal additional resources, whereas System B’s scaling requires exponential retraining and infrastructure expansion. Therefore, System A is more feasible to implement and more capable of achieving scalable impact.”

33.3 Key Guidelines for Writing Decisions

  • Be Concise – 3–5 sentences are sufficient. Avoid long narratives.

  • Be Evidence-Based – reference the Action Board, numerical data, timelines, or observed system logic.

  • Focus on Comparative Analysis – show why one system beats the other, not why it is good in isolation.

  • Avoid Personal or Stylistic Comments – no mention of speaking style, tone, or charisma.

  • End with Clarity – the winning system must be unmistakable.

33.4 Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It Fails ACAD Fix
Focusing on speeches Decisions must reflect systems, not delivery Reference the board and comparative logic
Listing too many factors Confuses teams and dilutes decisive points Only highlight 1–2 lenses that truly matter
Using vague language “Slightly better” is unclear Be absolute: “System A is superior in X and Y”
Omitting justification Teams cannot learn from feedback Always include board-based or evidence-based reasoning

Takeaway: A well-written ACAD decision is crisp, comparative, and evidence-backed, reflecting the culture of measurable impact, scalability, and operational realism that defines ACAD.

PART XIV – Training & Preparation

Training and preparation are critical to success in ACAD. Unlike traditional debates, ACAD requires skills in systems thinking, operational design, visual communication, and comparative analysis. This section outlines a structured training pathway for beginners, intermediates, and advanced teams.

34. Beginner Track

The Beginner Track is designed for students new to ACAD or to debating in general. The focus is on building foundational skills in understanding systems, mapping solutions, and presenting them visually.

34.1 System Mapping Drills

Objective: Learn to visualize a problem as a complete system with actors, flows, and feedback loops.

Exercise:

  • Choose a familiar sector (e.g., local tourism, school lunch program, transport).

  • Map:

  • Actors (who is involved)

  • Flows (how resources, information, or services move)

  • Constraints (what limits the system)

  • Incentives (what motivates actors)

  • Identify bottlenecks and failure points.

  • Discuss in pairs:

  • What is the root cause of inefficiency?

  • Which actor or process is most critical for change?

Tip: Start small; map one process before attempting the full system. Use sticky notes, whiteboards, or digital tools for flexibility.

34.2 Impact Chain Drills

Objective: Understand cause-effect relationships between actions and outcomes.

Exercise:

  • Take a small intervention in the system (e.g., launching a youth tourism training program).

  • Map the impact chain:

  • Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact

  • Identify measurable indicators for each step.

  • Ask:

  • Are these impacts realistic?

  • Could any step fail? How would you mitigate it?

Tip: Always tie outcomes back to root problems. Avoid surface-level impacts that don’t solve underlying issues.

34.3 Board Design Drills

Objective: Build skill in creating clear, logical, and evaluable Action Boards.

Exercise:

  • Take your system map and impact chain.

  • Layout an 8-section Action Board following the ACAD template:

  • Problem System Map

  • Intervention Architecture

  • Delivery Pathway

  • Resource & Constraint Model

  • Impact Logic Chain

  • Measurement Framework

  • Scaling & Replication Strategy

  • Risk & Failure Modes

  • Use arrows, color-coding, and hierarchy to show flows and interactions.

  • Pair teams to critique each other’s boards, focusing on:

  • Clarity

  • Logic

  • Completeness

  • Comparative visibility

Tip: Remember, visuals are for the Evaluator’s understanding, not decoration. If an Evaluator can’t trace your system at a glance, it’s too complex.

34.4 Integration Exercise: Mini ACAD Debate

Objective: Apply beginner skills in a controlled, low-stakes environment.

Exercise:

  • Assign a simple ACAD motion (e.g., “How can schools improve student participation in eco-tourism?”).

  • Each team maps the system, designs a small intervention, and creates a mini Action Board.

  • Conduct a 10–15 minute debate with shortened speeches:

  • System Lead 2 min

  • Design Lead 3 min

  • Delivery Lead 3 min

  • Impact Lead 2 min

  • Judges (trainers or peers) evaluate using simplified ACAD ballot: system clarity, delivery feasibility, impact credibility.

Outcome: Beginners learn how mapping, action boards, and comparison combine in ACAD.

Key Takeaways for Beginners

  • Start with small, manageable systems.

  • Focus on visual clarity and logical flow, not volume of ideas.

  • Always tie actions to measurable outcomes.

  • Practice both mapping systems and explaining them visually.

  • Early exposure to comparative analysis prepares for intermediate and advanced ACAD stages.

35. Advanced Track

The Advanced Track is for debaters who are comfortable with ACAD basics—mapping systems, designing interventions, building boards, and presenting impact chains. At this level, the focus shifts to strategic thinking, operational stress-testing, and system-level refinement.

These exercises ensure teams can survive scrutiny, adapt under pressure, and demonstrate comparative superiority in high-level debates.

35.1 Stress-Testing Models

Objective: Identify weak points, bottlenecks, and failure modes in your system before a debate.

Exercise:

  • Review your Action Board end-to-end.

  • Ask probing questions for each section:

  • System Map: Are any actors missing? Are flows realistic?

  • Intervention Architecture: Could components fail in real-world conditions?

  • Delivery Pathway: Are timelines feasible? Are responsibilities clear?

  • Impact Logic Chain: Are outcomes measurable and achievable?

  • Scaling & Replication: Will growth create new bottlenecks?

  • Document all potential failure points and update mitigation strategies.

Tip: Think like a real-world implementer: “If we launched tomorrow, what would break first, and how would we fix it?”

35.2 Red-Teaming

Objective: Strengthen systems by having opponents actively challenge them in controlled simulations.

Exercise:

  • Pair teams to act as internal critics (red team).

  • Red team attacks the system using ACAD-style solution attacks:

  • Missing actors or dependencies

  • Operational bottlenecks

  • Weak feedback loops

  • Unrealistic assumptions

  • Non-scalable design

  • Design team responds with evidence, redesigns, or mitigations.

Outcome: Teams learn to anticipate critiques and defend their system without relying on rhetoric, strengthening their comparative edge.

35.3 Rapid Redesign

Objective: Develop adaptability under time pressure, a critical skill for real-world problem-solving and ACAD debates.

Exercise:

  • Present a mock system attack (realistic scenario or opponent challenge).

  • Teams have 10–15 minutes to redesign the impacted section of their board:

  • Adjust intervention architecture

  • Recalculate delivery pathways

  • Update impact logic and scaling strategy

  • Present the redesigned board to a mini panel for feedback.

Tip: Focus on realistic, incremental changes, not complete overhauls. Judges will reward practical, evidence-backed redesigns over flashy but unrealistic fixes.

35.4 Integration Exercise: Advanced ACAD Simulation

  • Use a full ACAD motion with real-world complexity (e.g., “How can Rwanda’s tourism sector be transformed to maximize youth inclusion and sustainable impact?”).

  • Teams must present complete Action Boards, respond to peer attacks, undergo stress-testing, and adjust under rapid redesign conditions.

  • Judges use full ACAD ballot, focusing on system robustness, delivery feasibility, impact credibility, scalability, and comparative advantage.

Outcome: Teams graduate from board designers to strategic system architects, ready for high-stakes ACAD competitions.

Key Takeaways for Advanced Teams

  • Success at this level is less about what you present and more about how resilient your system is.

  • Anticipate attacks, stress-test aggressively, and redesign quickly under pressure.

  • Focus relentlessly on comparative advantages and evidence-backed scalability.

  • Advanced practice simulates real-world operational reality, making ACAD debates not just a game, but a training ground for actionable solutions.

PART XV – Common ACAD Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

Even the best debaters can fall into traps that reduce the clarity, impact, or credibility of their system. ACAD is unforgiving of shortcuts, sloppy reasoning, or reliance on rhetoric over operational logic. This section highlights the most common mistakes teams make, explains why they fail, and provides practical strategies to avoid them.

Strategic Pitfalls Table

Pitfall Why It Fails Strategic Correction
Overcomplicating the Board Logic becomes untraceable; evaluators lose the “thread” of the design. Focus on critical actors and modular flows. Use whitespace as a design tool.
Ignoring Root Causes Solutions address surface symptoms but leave underlying systems broken. Use Impact Logic Chains; ask if the problem persists if the intervention is removed.
Over-Reliance on Rhetoric Professional evaluators penalize flair that masks operational gaps. Let the board’s logic speak; reference evidence-backed data for every claim.
Unrealistic Delivery Plans The system collapses under the weight of institutional/logistical reality. Map timelines step-by-step; assign clear ownership for every component.
Ignoring Scaling Constraints Beautiful pilots often fail when extended to national or regional levels. Design replication strategies; identify geographic and resource limitations early.
Weak Contextual Evidence “Copy-paste” data lacks credibility in highly specific local environments. Use locally valid data; justify adaptations of external models to local reality.
Redundant Rebuttals Repeating speech points instead of collapsing comparisons wastes strategic time. Focus on 1–2 decisive lenses; tie every rebuttal back to impact and feasibility.
Misusing Cross-Ex Rhetorical traps miss the chance to probe structural system weaknesses. Use cross-ex to clarify assumptions and stress-test delivery bottlenecks.
Neglecting Indicators Vague outcomes prevent rigorous comparative evaluation. Define measurable indicators and verification sources on the Action Board.

Key Takeaways

  • ACAD rewards clarity, logic, and operational realism, not style or rhetoric.

  • Always think from the Evaluator’s perspective: can this system work, scale, and survive stress?

  • Preparation is about stress-testing your board, evidence, and assumptions repeatedly.

  • Mistakes are often avoidable through practice, peer review, and iterative redesign.

45. Action Board Template

Every ACAD solution should be presented using the same universal structure, so evaluators can compare systems fairly and efficiently.

Below is the official ACAD Action Board template, broken into 8 essential sections, with a description of what to include in each. Teams can adapt formatting for visual clarity, but content order and logic must remain consistent.

Section 1 – Problem System Map

Purpose: Clearly show the current system and its challenges.

Include:

  • Key actors (people, organizations, institutions)

  • Flows (resources, information, money, services)

  • Bottlenecks and failures

  • Feedback loops and incentives

Example (Tourism Youth Participation):

  • Actors: youth, tourism boards, hotels, training institutions

  • Flow: youth → training → tourism jobs → revenue

  • Bottleneck: limited training programs; incentive misalignment between hotels and youth

  • Feedback loop: youth leave sector → skill gap worsens → tourism service quality drops

Section 2 – Intervention Architecture

Purpose: Present how your solution works.

Include:

  • Components/modules of the intervention

  • How components interact

  • Ownership and accountability

Example:

  • Youth training hubs → mentorship programs → internship placements

  • Hotels provide practical training → tourism boards monitor progress

  • Clear ownership: Youth councils manage hubs; hotels manage internships

Section 3 – Delivery Pathway

Purpose: Show step-by-step implementation.

Include:

  • Timeline and sequence of activities

  • Responsible actors

  • Operational workflow

Example:

  • Month 1–2: Recruit youth participants

  • Month 2–6: Run training modules

  • Month 6–12: Internships in hotels

  • Responsible: Youth councils, tourism boards, hotel HR teams

Section 4 – Resource & Constraint Model

Purpose: Identify what is required to implement and potential challenges.

Include:

  • Skills and human resources

  • Budget and funding

  • Infrastructure requirements

  • Legal/regulatory/social constraints

Example:

  • Trainers: 20 certified guides

  • Budget: $50,000 for 1 year

  • Infrastructure: 5 training centers

  • Constraint: local travel limitations, language barriers

Section 5 – Impact Logic Chain

Purpose: Link activities to measurable impact.

Include:

  • Activities → outputs → outcomes → impact

  • Indicators for each stage

Example:

  • Activity: Youth training

  • Output: 100 youth trained

  • Outcome: 60% employed in tourism

  • Impact: Increased youth income + improved sector service quality

Section 6 – Measurement Framework

Purpose: Show how impact is monitored.

Include:

  • Indicators

  • Data sources

  • Monitoring methods

Example:

  • Indicator: Employment rate after training

  • Data source: Hotel HR records, youth surveys

  • Method: Quarterly reports + independent audit

Section 7 – Scaling & Replication Strategy

Purpose: Show how the solution grows beyond the pilot.

Include:

  • Geographic scaling

  • Population scaling

  • Institutional scaling

Example:

  • Expand from Kigali to Northern Province after 1 year

  • Include other youth demographics

  • Partner with national tourism boards for institutional support

Section 8 – Risk & Failure Modes

Purpose: Identify what can go wrong and mitigation strategies.

Include:

  • Operational, financial, human, political, or social risks

  • Strategies to prevent or mitigate failure

Example:

  • Risk: Low youth enrollment

  • Mitigation: Outreach campaigns, incentives, partnership with schools

  • Risk: Hotel resistance

  • Mitigation: Demonstrate ROI, provide recognition

Key Guidelines for Using the Action Board

  • Use visuals, color-coding, and arrows for clarity.

  • Keep each section concise and focused.

  • Ensure the flow is logical: Problem → Solution → Delivery → Impact → Scaling → Risks.

  • Always link components to evidence and realistic feasibility.

PART XVI – Ethics & Responsibility

ACAD is not just a debating format—it is a platform for responsible, evidence-based, and community-focused solution design. This section ensures that teams understand the ethical obligations that come with proposing real-world interventions.

49. Ethics in Applied Debating

ACAD debates often deal with real systems and real people, so ethical considerations are non-negotiable. Teams must avoid harm, misrepresentation, and overpromising.

1. Avoiding Community Harm

  • Solutions must respect local communities, their culture, and norms.

  • Example: A youth tourism hub must not displace other community programs or exploit labor.

  • Always ask: Could this solution inadvertently harm the community it’s meant to help?

2. Preventing Exploitation

  • Teams must avoid using stakeholders or youth as mere case studies without benefit.

  • Example: Asking youth to participate in pilots without training, payment, or mentorship is unethical.

3. Responsible Use of Data

  • Only use accurate, verified, and locally contextual data.

  • Avoid using foreign reports or imported solutions without adaptation.

  • Maintain transparency about assumptions and potential biases.

4. Avoiding False Scalability Claims

  • Teams must not exaggerate the reach or feasibility of their systems.

  • Example: Claiming a hub can train 10,000 youth in 3 months without resources is a violation.

  • Evaluators will penalize overpromised systems that cannot realistically be delivered.

5. Integrity in Presentation

  • Evidence must be cited accurately; assumptions clearly stated.

  • Avoid rhetorical tricks or misleading visuals to appear stronger than reality.

Ethics Checklist for ACAD Teams

Question Yes/No
Does this solution respect local communities and norms?
Are all stakeholders benefiting fairly from participation?
Is all data verifiable and contextually relevant?
Are scalability and impact claims realistic?
Is the presentation honest, clear, and evidence-backed?

Takeaway: ACAD is solution-focused, evidence-driven, and real-world oriented. Ethical lapses undermine not only your team’s credibility but also the integrity of the debate and the potential real-world impact. Teams that embed ethics from day one are more likely to design feasible, sustainable, and respected systems.

PART XVIII – Tournament & Scaling Rules

ACAD is designed to be scalable, from a classroom exercise to national or international competitions. This section standardizes how tournaments are run, ensuring consistency, fairness, and high-quality evaluation.

50. Tournament Operations

Running an ACAD tournament requires attention to space, equipment, timing, and judge preparation.

1. Room Setup

  • Seating: Teams sit facing each other, with a clear view of the evaluator(s).

  • Space for Action Boards: Each team must have enough room to display their board. Boards should be visible to all judges.

  • Audience: If spectators are allowed, ensure no obstruction of visual boards.

2. Screen / Board Requirements

  • Projector or large monitor for digital boards, OR

  • Printed boards mounted on easels or walls.

  • Boards must be legible from a distance, with clear sections and color-coded flows.

  • Teams should bring all materials needed (markers, laser pointers, handouts).

3. Timing

  • Official ACAD timing: 4–5–5–3 minutes per speaker, plus 4-minute cross-examination per team.

  • Warning system: Timer visible to speakers or discreet signals from a moderator.

  • Flexibility: Minor adjustments allowed for time zone or multi-room events, but ratios must remain.

4. Judge Briefing

  • Evaluators must understand ACAD-specific rules, including:

  • Evaluation lenses: System, Design, Delivery, Impact, Scalability, Comparative Analysis

  • What to ignore: rhetoric, style, charisma

  • How to score and write decisions: use official ballot template

  • Ethics expectations: adherence to responsible solution design

  • Judges should have access to Action Boards prior to scoring to review system logic.

Scaling ACAD

  • Single-room / local tournaments: 2–6 teams, manual timing, small panel of evaluators

  • Regional / national tournaments: Multiple rooms, standardized Action Board display, digital submissions for cross-verification

  • International tournaments: Pre-submitted boards, digital collaboration, multi-evaluator scoring panels, live streaming optional

Key Principle: Scaling must not compromise board clarity, timing integrity, or evaluation fairness.

51. Global Standardisation Rules

For ACAD to be adopted and recognized internationally, tournaments, teams, judges, and motions must comply with a universal set of rules. This ensures fairness, clarity, and credibility, and allows ACAD to scale across countries and regions.

1. Format Compliance

  • Mandatory structure: All ACAD debates must use the 4–5–5–3 timing, with 4 speakers per team.

  • Action Board usage: All teams must present Action Boards following the universal 8-section template.

  • Cross-examination: Mandatory 4-minute cross-ex per team, focused on system logic, feasibility, and scaling.

  • Comparative evaluation: Debates must be judged on System, Design, Delivery, Impact, Scalability, and Comparative Analysis.

Rationale: Deviations dilute the ACAD methodology and compromise comparability of debates worldwide.

2. Motion Approval

  • Official ACAD motions must be phrased in the challenge format: “How can stakeholders design scalable systems to [achieve X] in [context Y]?”

  • Motion approval process:

  • Submitted to a Global ACAD Motion Committee.

  • Checked for clarity, scope, and feasibility.

  • Approved motions are registered in the official ACAD motion database.

  • Scope rules: Motions must allow multiple solution approaches while remaining specific enough for measurable impact.

Tip: Avoid ambiguous or purely ideological motions—ACAD focuses on real-world solutions, not abstract debate.

3. Certification of Judges

  • Eligibility: Evaluators must demonstrate understanding of ACAD principles, including:

  • Comparative analysis

  • Evidence-backed evaluation

  • Action Board interpretation

  • Ethics in applied debating

  • Certification process:

  • Complete Global ACAD Judge Training Program (online or in-person).

  • Pass a written and practical evaluation, including scoring sample debates.

  • Receive official ACAD Judge Certification, valid for a defined period (e.g., 2 years).

  • Responsibilities of certified judges:

  • Follow the Impact Panel Manual and official ballot.

  • Avoid style, charisma, or rhetoric bias.

  • Ensure ethical compliance and fair comparative evaluation.

Note: Only certified judges can officiate regional or international ACAD tournaments.

Key Takeaways for Global Standardisation

  • Uniformity in format, timing, and roles ensures ACAD remains scalable and comparable.

  • Motions must be vetted for clarity, context, and actionable challenge.

  • Certified judges guarantee fairness, reliability, and integrity of evaluation.

  • Adhering to these rules protects the ACAD brand and facilitates international adoption.

PART XIX – ACAD Glossary

52. ACAD Vocabulary

Roles & Participants

  • System Lead (SL): Introduces the problem system, maps actors and flows, frames the design challenge.

  • Design Lead (DL): Presents the intervention architecture, explains component interactions, defends design logic.

  • Delivery Lead (DEL): Shows operational feasibility, implementation timeline, workflow, and responsibilities.

  • Impact Lead (IL): Compares impact models, evaluates scalability, measurement credibility, and collapses comparative advantage.

Key ACAD Concepts

  • Intervention Architecture: The design of the solution including components, interactions, and ownership.

  • Delivery Pathway: Step-by-step implementation plan with responsibilities and workflow.

  • Impact Logic Chain: Sequence linking activities → outputs → outcomes → measurable impact.

  • Measurement Framework: Metrics, data sources, and monitoring methodology to validate outcomes.

  • Scaling Strategy: Plan to expand solution geographically, institutionally, or across populations.

  • Risk & Failure Modes: Potential points of failure and strategies for mitigation.

Comparative Evaluation Terms

  • Reach: Number of people or institutions affected by the system.

  • Depth: Magnitude of change or benefit delivered per participant.

  • Sustainability: Ability of the system to maintain operations and outcomes over time.

  • Replicability: Ease with which the system can be reproduced in different contexts.

  • Institutional Alignment: Degree to which the solution integrates with existing structures or policies.

ACAD Debate Terminology

  • ACAD Motion: Official debate challenge phrased as: “How can stakeholders design scalable systems to [achieve X] in [context Y]?”

  • Cross-Examination: Timed session where teams question assumptions, feasibility, and scaling logic of the opposing solution.

  • Comparative Collapse: The Impact Lead’s synthesis that reduces all arguments to decisive system advantages.

  • Red-Teaming: Stress-testing your own solution for weaknesses before debate.

  • Evidence Types: Academic research, industry reports, operational data, pilot studies, or locally collected data.

Ethics & Standards

  • Responsible Solution Design: Solutions that avoid community harm, exploitation, or false claims.

  • Transparency: Clearly stating assumptions, limitations, and methodology.

  • Integrity: Accurate representation of evidence and outcomes; avoiding misrepresentation.
